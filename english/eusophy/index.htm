Basic Concepts in Logic 1

Intellection is the activity of knowing the objective world, and intellect (human intelligence) is the ability to know the objective world. Therefore, the objective world is the totality of the intellectual objects. An intellectual object is called an entity if it is meaningful to ask whether the object exists. For instance, ''UFOs'' and ''Peter'' (in ''The Tail of Peter Rabbit'') are entities. An intellectual object is called an event if it is meaningful to ask whether the object occurs. For instance, ''UFOs are round'' and ''Peter eats a radish'' are events, as well as their parts ''are round,'' ''eats a radish,'' and ''eats.'' Not all but many intellectual objects may be expressed by language. Linguistic expressions referring to entities are called nominals, while those referring to events are called declaratives. Observation of language and introspection show that the intellectual objects are divided into entities and events. Observation of language also shows that each event is either an attribute of an entity (and the attribute itself), or a relation between entities (and the relation itself). For instance, the event ''UFOs are round'' is an attribute of the entity ''UFOs'' and the event ''are round'' is the attribute itself. The event ''Peter eats a radish'' is a relation between the entities ''Peter'' and ''radish,'' and the event ''eats'' is the relation itself. The event ''Peter eats a radish'' is also an attribute of the entity ''Peter'' and the event ''eats a radish'' is the attribute itself, which is also an attribute of the entity ''radish,'' showing that someone eats ''radish,'' and the event ''eats'' is also the attribute itself. Therefore, each event A may involve some entities a1, ..., an for some positive integer n, so that A is sometimes denoted by A(a1, ..., an). Here a1, ..., an may not be all of the entities involved. For instance, if ''Peter eats a radish,'' ''Peter,'' and ''radish'' are denoted by A, b, and c, respectively, then A may also be denoted by A(b), A(c), A(b,c), and A(c,b).

Let A1, ..., An, and B denote events, where n is a nonnegative integer. Then the following five expressions are equivalent by definition:

  1. If all of A1, ..., An occur, then B occurs.
  2. The tuple (A1, ..., An) implies B, that is, A1, ..., An together imply B.
  3. A1, ..., An together are connected to B by causality (causation, causal relation, cause-effect relation).
  4. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  5. A1, ..., An => B.

This definition in particular says that the symbolic expression A1, ..., An => B in 5 should be read as in 1 through 4, especialy as ''A1, ..., An together imply B.'' By definition, the expressions 1 through 5 are also equivalent to the following one, provided n > 1:

  1. A1 implies B under A2, ..., An.

Because of these definitions, the expressions 1 through 6 are also equivalent to the expressions obtained by arbitrary permutation of A1, ..., An. To understand causality in Hypothesis 1 below means finding out the reason why A1, ..., An => B (A1, ..., An together imply B) for some tuple A1, ..., An, and B of events. When n = 0 in the expression A1, ..., An => B, it is denoted by () => B, and B is said to be inevitable or, in religious context, B is said to have been determined by Providence (God's will). For instance, the event ''x is x'' is inevitable for an arbitrary entity ''x.'' Obviously, if B is inevitable, then A1, ..., An => B for every tuple A1, ..., An of events. If B is not inevitable, we say that B is evitable or avoidable. A law is an event which is supposed to be inevitable.

Examples

Example 1
Let ''x'' denote an arbitrary entity and let A(x) and B(x) denote the event ''x is a planet'' and the event ''x goes around the sun,'' respectively. Then Newton has shown that A(x) implies B(x) under the law of universal gravitation, the law of motion, and certain implicit laws, such as the law of equlity between intellectual objects.
Example 2
Let A denote the event ''Humankind has emitted too much CO2 since the industrial revolution,'' and let B denote the event ''The global temperature is rising.'' Then climate scientists assert that A implies B under certain laws A1, ..., An, while A1, ..., An together do not imply B, hence B is avoidable by deleting A, that is, by achieving net zero carbon dioxide emissions until 2050. On the other hand, some people assert that B is inevitable, and so any tuple of events imply B, that is, B is not avoidable by any means. Especially, some people assert, in religious context, that it has been determined by Providence.

Basic Concepts in Logic 2

Observation of language shows that there are certain operations on the events. Each operation assigns an event to each tuple A1, ..., An of events, for a positive integer n called the arity of the operation. If the operation is denoted by F, then the event which F assigns to A1, ..., An is denoted by F(A1, ..., An). For each event A, -A denotes the negation of A. The symbol ''−'' stands for the expression ''It is not the case that'' and therefore is prepositive (incidentally, it should be postpositive in the Japanese word order). If A denotes, say, the event ''UFOs are round,'' then -A is the event ''It is not the case that UFOs are round,'' which is equivalent to the event ''UFOs are not round.'' For each pair A, B of events, A & B denotes the conjunction of A and B. For instance, if A and B denote the events ''Peter eats a radish'' and ''Peter eats a lettuce,'' respectively, then A & B is the event ''Peter eats a radish and Peter eats a lettuce,'' which is equivalent to the event ''Peter eats a radish and a lettuce.'' The negation operation assigns each event A its negation -A. It is a unary operation and denoted by the symbol ''-,'' hence -(A) = -A. The conjunction operation assigns each pair A, B of events their conjunction A & B. It is a binary operation and denoted by the symbol &, hence &(A,B) = A & B. The negation operation and the conjunction operation are basic ones. There is a variety of operations, because operations can be composed. For instance, composing the negation operation and the conjunction operation, we obtain the operation which assigns each pair A, B of events the event -(-A & -B), although it is equivalent to the basic event ''A or B.''

Let F be an operation of arity n. Then F is called a deduction if A1, ..., An => F(A1, ..., An), that is, if A1, ..., An together imply F(A1, ..., An), for each tuple A1, ..., An of events. For instance, the conjunction operation & is a deduction, while the negation operation ''-'' is not a deduction, because ''x is x'' does not imply -(''x is x''), or more generally, no inevitable event implies -(''x is x'').

Let C be a collection of events. Then, for each operation F, F(C) denotes the collection of all events F(A1, ..., An) for all tuples A1, ..., An of events in C. An event B is said to be generated by C or C-generated if the following holds:

There is a sequence A1, ..., An (for some positive integer n) of events such that An is equivalent to B, and for each integer k from 1 through n, Ak satisfies one of the following two conditions:

  1. Ak is a member of C (indeed, A1 is a member of C, because it does not satisfy the following condition).
  2. Ak is a member of Fk({A1, ..., Ak-1}) for some deduction Fk, where {A1, ..., Ak-1} denotes the collection of A1, ..., Ak-1.

Obviously, if B is generated by C, then it is also generated by every collection larger than C.

If Ak satisfies the condition 1, then there is an event Ck in C such that Ak = Ck. If Ak satisfies the condition 2, then there is a tuple A1', ..., Ak' such that Ak = Fk(A1', ..., Ak'), and the numbers 1', ..., k' are less than k. Therefore, if B is C-generated, then we can inductively construct a tree which illustrates how B is C-generated. We call it a C-tree for B. Let L be a collection of laws. If L has the following property called omnipotence, then we say that L is omnipotent:

The following two expressions are equivalent for each tuple A1, ..., An, B of events:

  1. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  2. B is generated by the union L ∪ {A1, ..., An}.

If L has the following property called omniscience, then we say that L is omniscient:

The following two expressions are equivalent for each tuple A1, ..., An, B of events:

  1. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  2. There is an (L ∪ {A1, ..., An})-tree for B.

Though different in appearance, omnipotence and omniscience are equivalent, as shown above. If L has these properties, we say that L rules (governs) causality.

Answer to Questions 1 and 3, and Much More

As a mathematician, I was able to analyze the mathematical models constructed in “Answer to Question 2,” and, in particular, to prove the following two propositions about each universe:

Propositions about the universe.
Proposition 1. The universe has its own causality among its events.
Proposition 2. There is a collection of laws which rules the causality. Obviously, if a collection of laws rules causality, then so does the collection obtained by adding laws to it. Therefore, there exists a unique and maximal collection of laws which rules causality.

Thus, we are naturally led to the following six definitions, which contain answers to Questions 1 and 3:

Definitions about God.
Definition 1. God of the universe is the unique and maximal collection of laws which rules its causality, which exists by Proposition 2.
Definition 2. God's omnipotence is that of the collection.
Definition 3. God's omniscience is that of the collection.
Definition 4. God's omnipresence is the property of the causality that it applies to all events in the whole space-time.
Definition 5. Providence (God's will) is the causality.

Then, as shown above, God's omnipotence and God's omniscience are equivalent. Because of Proposition 2 and Definition 1, God subsumes all laws, such as the logical ones, the law of universal gravitation, and probably the law of organic evolution.

There are universes where no human exists, such as physical one and mathematical one, that is, the ranges of intellectual objects that interest physicists and mathematicians. For other universes where humans exist, we can define as follows:

Definition 6. God's omnibenevolence is the property of Providence (causality), or rather the underlying law of organic evolution, that humankind was born and has survived so far under it.

This definition may allow us to forget about the Problem of Evil, because the law of organic evolution is not necessarily benevolent to individuals.

In each monothetic religion, the deity is, among other things, an object of both reverence and dread. The God defined in Definition 1 may well be an object of dread for anyone, because the laws that govern (rule) causality are profound and deeply involved in every aspect of daily life. However, God is unlikely to be an object of reverence, since we humans seem capable of reverence only toward personified beings perceived as exalted in spirit. This may appear to be a shortcoming of Definition 1 for those who have guided their actions through reverence and dread toward a transcendent, life-bearing deity. Yet this is more than compensated by a significant advantage: Definition 1 has given rise to eusophy, namely, a system that can generate universally respectable and trustworthy norms of action, grounded in the supreme good articulated in Conclusion 2. Such norms, and those who establish, spread, and practice them for the survival of humankind, are worthy of our respect. Moreover, God is connected with the deities of monothetic religions through the essence of religion articulated in Observation 4. Furthermore, its existence can be logically demonstrated, as stated in Proposition 2 and Definition 1. Providence is naturally defined by Definition 5, and its principal attributes (omnipotence, omniscience, omnipresence, and omnibenevolence) are rigorously derived through Definitions 2, 3, 4, and 6. The Problem of Evil can be avoided, as previously discussed. Omnipotence, omniscience, and Definition 5 show that God governs Providence and is capable of explaining it; thus, we may say that God is rational. Definition 5 also shows that Providence determines whether a tuple of events implies another (a similar formulation has long been used in Christian theology). Above all, God is compatible with science, as shown below. This compatibility was crucial for me in arriving at Conclusion 1.

The compatibility of God, as defined in Definition 1, with evolutionism can be demonstrated in the following way. Evolutionism asserts that

(8)The law of organic evolution created all species, including humankind.

Probably, God subsumes the law of organic evolution, as stated above. Therefore, we may also assert that

(9)God created all species.

In other words, (8) is a refinement of (9). Thus, God, or rather creationism is compatible with evolutionism. Moreover, intelligent design theory is compatible with evolutionism, since God is intelligent in the sense of being omniscient, as defined in Definition 3.

More generally, God, as defined in Definition 1, is compatible with science for the following three reasons.

  1. Scientists implicitly believe in God in that they each pursue laws that govern aspects of causality (Providence) as articulated in Observation 1 and Definition 5.
  2. The laws they discover may, in fact, constitute parts of God.
  3. Scientific discovery may be regarded as a revelation of God through the scientists themselves, for their intellectual endeavors also follow Providence (causality).
