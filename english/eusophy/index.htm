§1. Introduction

This article is a reorganized and abridged translation of its Japanese counterpart, and it amplifies the non-mathematical part of my paper Foundations of Eusophy: A mathematical concept of God for the reconciliation of religion and science, hereafter referred to as MP (mother paper). It, in turn, was born of my earlier paper Mathematical Noology: Intellectual machines, logic, tongues and algebra, hereafter referred to as FP (fore-paper).

Anyone should know the non-mathematical part of MP, or at least the spirit of the new intellectual deed proposed and called eusophy there, because it is crucial both for humankind and for individuals. Namely, it leads to an answer to the question

(1)What should anyone do?

and it aims at

(2)The pursuit of a way for humankind to survive

in view of the following alarm based on climate science.

(3)The global temperature rise may become a threat to survival of humankind in the not too distant future, if our action against it is not enough.

Thus, I have decided to write this article in order to introduce eusophy to worldwide general readership.

It is worth noting here that language reflects the mind, but the mind is also shaped by language. When the words we use are misleading, our thoughts and actions may be misled as well. For instance, the widespread term global warming may unconsciously evoke the idea of a pleasantly mild climate, leading some to underestimate the severity of the crisis and to neglect necessary action. Hence my choice to write global temperature rise above.

Eusophy subsumes various intellectual deeds, because survival of humankind depends on the state of the whole world in a long period. It also transcends various boundaries, both visible and invisible, such as national borders, because the alarm (3) is supplemented as follows:

(4)The threat posed by rising global temperatures recognizes no boundaries, not even national ones.

Both survival in (3) and survive in (2) imply continuation as a species. Therefore, the following taxonomical knowledge is the starting point of eusophy:

(5)Humankind is but one among many species.

This insight, which I call The Declaration of Humankind's Origin, will be emphasized together with evolutionism and climate science wherever appropriate, for the following three reasons:

  1. Even in solving problems about humans, it is helpful to consider their nature as a kind of organisms (animals, plants, and bacteria).
  2. The law of organic evolution governs how species survive.
  3. The mainstay of society still does not seem to be responding adequately to the alarm (3).

Basic concepts in logic

Intellection is the activity of knowing the objective world, and intellect is the ability to know the objective world. Therefore, the objective world is the totality of the intellectual objects. The intellectual objects are divided into entities and events. An intellectual object is called an entity if we can ask whether it exists. For instance, UFs and Peter (in The Tail of Peter Rabbit) are entities. An intellectual object is called an event if we can ask whether it occurs. For instance, UFs are round and Peter eats a radish are events, as well as are round, eats a radish, and eats. Not all but many intellectual objects may be expressed by language. Expressions of entities are called nominals, while those of events are called declaratives. Observation of language shows that each event is either an attribute of an entity and the attribute itself or a relation between entities and the relation itself. For instance, the event UFs are round is an attribute of the entity UFs and the event are round is the attribute itself. The event Peter eats a radish is a relation between the entities Peter and radish, and the event eats is the relation itself. The event Peter eats a radish is also an attribute of the entity Peter and the event eats a radish is the attribute itself, which is also an attribute of the entity radish, showing that someone eats radish, and the event eats is also the attribute itself. Therefore, each event A may involve some entities a1, ..., ak for some positive integer k, when A is sometimes denoted by A(a1, ..., ak). Here a1, ..., ak may not be all of the entities involved. For instance, if Peter eats a radish, Peter, and radish are denoted by A, b, and c, respectively, then A may also be denoted by A(b), A(c), A(b,c), and A(c,b).

Let A1, ..., An, and B denote events, where n is a nonnegative integer. Then the following four expressions are equivalent by definition:

  1. Whenever A1, ..., An occur, B occurs.
  2. A1, ..., An imply B.
  3. A1, ..., An is connected to B by causality (causation, cause-effect relation).
  4. A1, ..., An => B.
To understand causality in Hypothesis 1 below means finding out the reason why A1, ..., An => B holds for some set A1, ..., An, and B of events. When n = 0 in the expression A1, ..., An => B, then it is denoted by () => B, and B is said to be inevitable or, in religious context, B is said to have been determined by Providence (God's will). For instance, the event x is x is inevitable for an arbitrary entity x. Obviously, if B is inevitable, then A1, ..., An => B holds for every set A1, ..., An of events. If B is not inevitable, we say that B is evitable or avoidable. By definition, the expressions 1 through 4 are also equivalent to the following, provided n > 1:

  1. A1 implies B under the laws A2, ..., An.

Because of these definitions, the expressions 1 through 5 are also equivalent to the expressions obtained by permutation of A1, ..., An.

Examples

Example 1
Let x denote an arbitrary entity and let A(x) and B(x) denote the event x is a planet and the event x goes around the sun, respectively. Then Newton has shown that A(x) implies B(x) under the law of universal gravitation, the law of motion, and certain implicit laws.
Example 2
Let A denote the event The humankind has emitted too much CO2 since the industrial revolution, and let B denote the event The global temperature is rising. Then climate scientists assert that A implies B under certain laws A1, ..., An, that is, A, A1, ..., An => B holds, and that A1, ..., An => B does not holds, hence B is avoidable by deleting A, that is, by achieving net zero carbon dioxide emissions until 2050. On the other hand, some people assert that B is inevitable, that is, () => B holds and so A1, ..., An => B holds for every set A1, ..., An of events, hence B is by no means avoidable or, in religious context, it has been determined by Providence.

Hypotheses

Primitive humans initiated a foundational intellectual activity, whose purpose was to understand causality in order to survive as organisms.
Hypothesis 2.
Humankind survived as a species thanks to the activity.
Hypothesis 3.
The activity gradually changed and ramified into some branches.
Hypothesis 4.
Over time, four major branches evolved into what we now recognize as science, logic, ethics, and religion.

Scientists each pursue laws which rule (govern) aspects of causality.

Religion is essentially a system of speculations and prayers, both on causality, and the acceptance of their outcomes.
Observation 5 about organisms.
Each organism is a system for continuing life, which is the process of reproducing its parts, including itself.
Observation 6 about humankind.
Although humankind is but one among many species by (5), it is distinguished by its high intelligence, and its intelligent activity is recorded in civilization, which is defined as the totality of human knowledge.
Observation 7 about survival of humankind.
Continuation of the biosphere is necessary for survival of humankind, because humankind is a member of the biosphere by (5) and all its members are connected by the food cycle (the composite of the food chain and the carbon cycle), the symbiotic relationship, and the habitat bioprovision (the phenomenon in which part or all of an organism’s habitat is created and provided by other organisms).

§2. Intellect, Universe, God, and Science

Although I do not adhere to any established religion, I sometimes find myself drawn to religious spaces. At a Christmas Eve service in 2012, I encountered a verse from the Gospel of John that reads:

All things were made by the Word.

This verse astonished me, for reasons I explain below.

Since 1992, I have been studying intellect, which I define as the ability to know the objective world. This inquiry was motivated by the question What is humanity? and guided by the following proposition:

(6)Humankind is a species distinguished from other species by its intellect.

Intellect is inseparably linked to language, in the following sense:

(7)Intellect evolved because humankind acquired language.

This evolutionary process may be outlined as follows:

  1. In early human societies, intellect existed prior to language.
  2. Language first emerged as a tool for communication with others.
  3. It then became a tool for internal communication.
  4. Consequently, it developed into a medium for thought and memory.
  5. By transmitting knowledge and mode of thought to future generations, it has served as a genetic analogue for the evolution of intellect.
  6. Thus, intellect evolved.

This entire process may be encapsulated in the above phrase All things were made by the Word. Therefore, I was astonished, because it seemed to me that John had already known everything I had just described.

The preaching, however, was disappointing. According to it, Word in the Gospel of John does not mean language, but means God's words such as Let there be light for creating the heaven and the earth. With some hope, however, I turned to the World Wide Web, found that the Wisdom of Solomon has something to do with Word, and found out the following two verses in it:

As for wisdom, what she is, and how she came up, I will tell you, and will not hide mysteries from you: but will seek her out from the beginning of her nativity, and bring the knowledge of her into light, and will not pass over the truth.
God hath granted me to speak as I would, and to conceive as is meet for the things that are given me: because it is he that leadeth unto wisdom, and directeth the wise.

These verses seemed mostly adequate to a prologue and an epilogue respectively of a certain section of FP, which amplifies (7). The latter, however, also seemed inadequate to FP, because I had long felt that science was incompatible with God, while noology in FP is the science of intellectual phenomena.

The above inadequacy, however, was the beginning of a fortunate chain of unexpected events. Namely, it spotlighted the following in my mind:

Question 1: What is God?

and since they say that God is the omnipotent ruler of the universe, it switched on spotlight lamps in my mind one after another:

Question 2: What is the universe?

Question 3: What is the omnipotent rule over the universe?

Moreover, it soon flashed in my mind that we could neatly answer the questions by using logics (general logic and case logic) in FP.

Answer to Question 2: I have defined intellect as the ability to know the objective world. Therefore, the objective world is the totality of the intellectual objects. The intellectual objects are divided into entities and events. An intellectual object is called an entity if we can ask whether it exists. An intellectual object is called an event if we can ask whether it occurs. The objective world is vast. One works one's intellection only on some limited range of intellectual objects according to one's interest. The objective world consists of such limited ranges. Because of (7), we can observe such ranges through languages. Being a mathematical scientist, I could furthermore construct mathematical models of such ranges, on the basis of the observations. The models constitute my answer to Question 2. In terms of the models, the limited ranges are called the universes. Therefore, the objective world is a multiverse consisting of the universes.

Answer to Questions 1 and 3 and much more: Being a mathematician, I could analyze the mathematical models obtained by the Answer to Question 2, and could show in particular that the following holds for each universe:

Facts about the universe.
Fact 1. The universe has its own causality among its events.
Fact 2. The causality is ruled (governed) by a collection of laws, that is, events are connected by causality if and only if they are connected by laws in the collection. Obviously, if a collection of laws rules the causality, then so does the collection obtained by adding correct rules to it. Therefore, there exists unique and maximal collection of laws which rules causality.

Thus, we are naturally led to the following six definitions, which contain answers to Questions 1 and 3:

Definitions about God.
Definition 1. God of the universe is the unique and maximal collection of laws which rules its causality, which exists by Fact 2.
Definition 2. God's omnipotent rule over the universe is the rule over the causality.
Definition 3. God's omniscience is the capability of explaining causality by the laws constituting God.
Definition 4. God's omnipresence is the property of the causality that it applies to all events in the whole space-time.
Definition 5. Providence (God's will) is the causality.

Because of Fact 2 and Definition 1, God subsumes all correct laws, such as the logical ones, the law of universal gravitation, and probably the law of organic evolution.

There are universes where no human exists, such as physical one and mathematical one, that is, the ranges of intellectual objects that interest physicists and mathematicians. For other universes where humans exist, we can define as follows:

Definition 6. God's omnibenevolence is the property of Providence (causality), or rather the underlying law of organic evolution, that humankind was born and has survived so far under it.

This definition may allow us to forget about the Problem of Evil, because the law of organic evolution is not necessarily benevolent to individuals.

In each monothetic religion, the deity is, among other things, an object of both reverence and dread. The God defined in Definition 1 may well be an object of dread for anyone, because the laws that govern (rule) causality are profound and deeply involved in every aspect of daily life. However, God is unlikely to be an object of reverence, since we humans seem capable of reverence only toward personified beings perceived as exalted in spirit. This may appear to be a shortcoming of Definition 1 for those who have guided their actions through reverence and dread toward a transcendent, life-bearing deity. Yet this is more than compensated by a significant advantage: Definition 1 has given rise to eusophy, namely, a system that can generate universally respectable and trustworthy norms of action, grounded in the supreme good articulated in Conclusion 2. Such norms, and those who establish, spread, and practice them for the survival of humankind, are worthy of our respect. Moreover, God is connected with the deities of monothetic religions through the essence of religion articulated in Observation 4. Furthermore, its existence can be logically demonstrated, as stated in Fact 2 and Definition 1. Providence is naturally defined by Definition 5, and its principal attributes (omnipotence, omniscience, omnipresence, and omnibenevolence) are rigorously derived through Definitions 2, 3, 4, and 6. The Problem of Evil can be avoided, as previously discussed. Omnipotence, omniscience, and Definition 5 show that God governs Providence and is capable of explaining it; thus, we may say that God is rational. Definition 5 also shows that Providence determines whether one event implies another (a similar formulation has long been used in Christian theology). Above all, God is compatible with science, as shown below. This compatibility was crucial for me in arriving at Conclusion 1.

The compatibility of God with evolutionism, as defined in Definition 1, can be demonstrated in the following way. Evolutionism asserts that

(8)The law of organic evolution created all species, including humankind.

Probably, God subsumes the law of organic evolution, as stated above. Therefore, we may also assert that

(9)God created all species.

In other words, (8) is a refinement of (9). Thus, God, or rather creationism is compatible with evolutionism. Moreover, intelligent design theory is compatible with evolutionism, since God is intelligent in the sense of being omniscient, as defined in Definition 3.

More generally, God is compatible with science, as defined in Definition 1, for the following three reasons.

  1. Scientists implicitly believe in God in that they each pursue laws that govern aspects of causality (Providence) as articulated in Observation 1 and Definition 5.
  2. The laws they discover may, in fact, constitute parts of God.
  3. Scientific discovery may be regarded as a revelation of God through the scientists themselves, for their intellectual endeavors also follow Providence (causality).

The prologue of the introductory chapter of FP is the proverb Know thyself in ancient Greece, because it seems as proposing the question What is humanity? which motivates noology in FP. The proverb is sometimes supplemented as follows:

Know thyself, and thou shalt know the universe and God.

This seems as predicting Noology will lead to an understanding of the universe and God. In any case, the prediction has come true in the first draft of MP. Some readers have sent me useful comments on it. Among them are the following two from a physicist and a mathematician:

  1. Physicists have long been studying causality.
  2. This paper seems good for reconciling religion and science.

The former led me to Hypotheses 1 through 4, which together with the latter led me to Conclusion 1. Thus, MP has become complete to be the mother of this article.
