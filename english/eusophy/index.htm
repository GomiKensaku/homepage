0.1. Eusophy — Its Aim, Relevant Problems, and Insights

Anyone should know the non-mathematical part of ''FE,'' or at least the spirit of the new intellectual endeavor proposed and called Eusophy there, because it is crucial both for humankind and for individuals. Namely, it leads to an answer to what I call the Universal Question:

(1)What should anyone do?

(2)The pursuit of a way for humankind to survive

in view of the following Heating Alarm based on climate science:

(3)Global heating was caused by humanity's post-Industrial Revolution way of living, and may become a threat to survival of humankind in the not too distant future, unless humanity transform its way of living.

Thus, I have decided to write this article in order to introduce Eusophy to worldwide general readership.

It is worth noting the following:

(4)Language reflects the mind, but the mind is also shaped by language.

This has both merits and dangers. One of the dangers is that the mind can be misled by inappropriate terms — whether one's own or those of others. For instance, the widespread term ''global warming'' may unconsciously evoke the idea of a pleasantly mild climate, leading some to underestimate the severity of the crisis and to neglect necessary action. Hence my choice to use the term ''global heating'' in the Heating Alarm (3) above and throughout this article.

Eusophy is primarily the pursuit of human omniscience in service of the survival of humankind. It also encompasses a wide range of intellectual endeavors — from theoretical reflection to practical action. Even ''MP,'' ''MN,'' and ''FE'' are embraced within the scope of Eusophy. They deepen our understanding of the universe, intellect, and God. This understanding is to provide part of the foundations for theoretical reflection. The remaining part of the foundations is to be provided in this article through deepening our understanding of fundamental aspects of humanity other than intellect, such as values, emotion, volition, memory, life, death, happiness, and so on.

Eusophy also transcends various boundaries, both visible and invisible, such as national and sectarian borders, because the Heating Alarm (3) is supplemented as follows:

(5)The threat posed by global heating recognizes no boundaries.

Both ''survive'' in the Eusophic Aim (2) and ''survival'' in the Heating Alarm (3) imply ''continuation as a species.'' Therefore, the following taxonomical knowledge is the starting point of Eusophy:

(6)Humankind is but one among many species.

(7)Humankind is distinguished from other species by its intellect.

(9)Humankind has evolved a weakened sense of crisis and a tendency toward conflicting value systems.

These evolutionary and civilizational factors together help explain humanity's lack of urgency regarding global heating, and why effective countermeasures have been so slow to advance.

There is yet another factor that delays effective action against global heating. According to the Heating Alarm (3), humanity must transform its way of living for the sake of its own future. 0.2. Intellect and the Objective World

As for intellect — humankind's most admirable trait, as indicated in Insight (7) — we shall examine it in depth in §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language.'' Let us begin our preparation here.

Intellection is the activity of knowing the objective world, and intellect (HI, human intelligence) is the capacity to know that world. Accordingly, the objective world is the totality of intellectual objects.

An intellectual object is called an entity if it makes sense to ask whether it exists. For instance, ''UFOs'' and ''Peter'' (in ''The Tail of Peter Rabbit'') are entities. An intellectual object is called an event if it makes sense to ask whether it occurs. For instance, ''UFOs are round'' and ''Peter eats a radish'' are events, as well as their parts ''are round,'' ''eats a radish,'' and ''eats.'' An entity x exists if and only if the event ''x exists'' occurs.

Not all but many intellectual objects may be expressed by language. Linguistic expressions referring to entities are called nominals, while those referring to events are called declaratives.

Observation of language and introspection show that the intellectual objects are divided into entities and events. Observation of language also shows that each event is either an attribute or an act of an entity (and the attribute or the act itself) or a relation among entities (and the relation itself).

For instance, the event ''UFOs are round'' is an attribute of the entity ''UFOs'' and the event ''are round'' is the attribute itself. The event ''Peter eats a radish'' is a relation between the entities ''Peter'' and ''a radish,'' and the event ''eats'' is the relation itself. The event ''Peter eats a radish'' is also an act of the entity ''Peter'' and the event ''eats a radish'' is the act itself, which is also an attribute of the entity ''a radish,'' showing that ''a radish'' is eaten by someone, and the event ''eats'' is also the attribute itself.

Taken together, the above suggests that the essence of the objective world is the occurrence of events. However, we will soon see that the true essence is causality (causation, causal relation, cause-effect relation).

0.3. Causality and Necessity

Let A1, ..., An, and B denote events, where n is a nonnegative integer. Then the following six expressions are equivalent by definition:

  1. If all of A1, ..., An occur, then B occurs.
  2. The tuple (A1, ..., An) implies B, that is, A1, ..., An together imply B.
  3. A1, ..., An together are causally connected with B.
  4. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  5. A1 implies B under A2, ..., An.
  6. A1, ..., An ⇒ B.

If n = 0, then A1, ..., An should be understood not to exist, and similarly for A2, ..., An in the case n = 1. This definition in particular says that the symbolic expression A1, ..., An ⇒ B may be read as the expressions 1 through 5, especially as 2 or 3. It also says that the expressions 1 through 6 are equivalent to the expressions obtained by arbitrary permutation of A1, ..., An. ''To understand causality'' in Hypothesis 1 below means reasoning, that is, the act of pursuing the reason why A1, ..., An ⇒ B for some tuple (A1, ..., An, B) of events.

If n = 0 in the expression A1, ..., An ⇒ B, it is denoted by () ⇒ B. It means that B necessarily occurs, that is, C1, ..., Cm ⇒ B for every tuple (C1, ..., Cm) of events. This is because, if all of C1, ..., Cm occur, then, as n = 0, all of A1, ..., An obviously occur, and so B occurs. Therefore, if () ⇒ B, B is also said to be necessary. We may replace ''necessary'' by ''inevitable'' and ''unavoidable.'' In religious context, we may say that B has been determined by Providence (God's will). For instance, the event ''x is x'' is necessary for any entity x. A law is an event which is supposed to be necessary.

Let B be an event. If B is not necessary, we naturally say that B is unnecessary, evitable, or avoidable. If the negation -B of B is necessary, we say that B is impossible. Then, since -(-B) is equivalent to B, -B is impossible if and only if B is necessary. For instance, the event ''x is not x'' is impossible for any entity x. If B is not impossible, we naturally say that B is possible. If B is neither necessary nor impossible, that is, if B is unnecessary and possible, we say that B is contingent. Then, B is contingent if and only if -B is contingent. As will be shown by ''Proposition on Paradoxes'' in §1.6: ''Mistakes in Paradoxes,'' no event is both necessary and impossible. Therefore, every event is either unnecessary or possible. In other words, necessary events are possible, and impossible events are unnecessary. For instance, for any entity x, the event ''x is x'' is possible, and the event ''x is not x'' is unnecessary, or rather, the proof of the above proposition is based on the fact that ''x is x'' is possible.

1.3. Omnipotence and Omniscience

Observation of language shows that there exist certain operations on the events. Each operation assigns an event to each tuple (A1, ..., An) of events, for a positive integer n called the arity of the operation. If the operation is denoted by F, then the event which F assigns to (A1, ..., An) is denoted by F(A1, ..., An).

For each event A, -A denotes the negation of A. The symbol ''−'' stands for the expression ''It is not the case that'' and therefore is prepositive (incidentally, it should be postpositive in the Japanese word order). If A denotes, say, the event ''UFOs are round,'' then -A is the event ''It is not the case that UFOs are round,'' which is equivalent to the event ''UFOs are not round.'' For each pair (A, B) of events, A & B denotes the conjunction of A and B. For instance, if A and B denote the events ''Peter eats a radish'' and ''Peter eats a lettuce,'' respectively, then A & B is the event ''Peter eats a radish and Peter eats a lettuce,'' which is equivalent to the event ''Peter eats a radish and a lettuce.''

The negation operation assigns each event A its negation -A. It is a unary operation and denoted by the symbol ''-,'' hence -(A) = -A. The conjunction operation assigns each pair (A, B) of events their conjunction A & B. It is a binary operation and denoted by the symbol &, hence &(A,B) = A & B. The negation operation and the conjunction operation are basic ones. There exists a variety of operations, because operations can be composed. For instance, composing the negation operation and the conjunction operation, we obtain the operation which assigns each pair (A, B) of events the event -(-A & -B), although it is equivalent to the basic event ''A OR B.''

Let F be an n-ary operation on the events. Then F is called a deduction if A1, ..., An ⇒ F(A1, ..., An) for each tuple (A1, ..., An) of events. For instance, the conjunction operation & is a deduction, while the negation operation ''-'' is not a deduction, because ''x is x'' does not imply -(''x is x''), or more generally, no necessary event A satisfies A ⇒ -A, as will be shown by ''Proposition on Paradoxes'' and the logical laws in §1.6: ''Mistakes in Paradoxes.''

Let F be an n-ary operation on the events, and C be a collection of events. Then F(C) denotes the collection of all events F(A1, ..., An) for all tuples (A1, ..., An) of events in C. This notation is different from mathematical notation, where F(C) is denoted by F(Cn).

Let C be a collection of events. Then an event B is said to be generated by C if the following holds:

For some nonnegative integer n, there exists a sequence A0, ..., An of events such that An = B, and for each integer k from 0 through n, the event Ak satisfies one of the following two conditions:

  1. Ak belongs to C (indeed, A0 belongs to C, because it does not satisfy the following condition).
  2. Ak belongs to Fk({A0, ..., Ak-1}) for some deduction Fk, where {A0, ..., Ak-1} denotes the collection of A0, ..., Ak-1.

Such a sequence is called an explanation of B by C. Obviously, the following holds:

Proposition on Generation and Explanation
Let C be a collection of events, and B be an event. Then B is generated by C if and only if there exists an explanation of B by C. If B is generated by C, then it is also generated by every collection of events containing C.

Suppose Ak satisfies condition 2 above and k' is the arity of the deduction Fk in it. Then there exists a tuple (B1, ..., Bk') of events in {A0, ..., Ak-1} such that Ak = Fk(B1, ..., Bk'), which implies B1, ..., Bk' ⇒ Ak, because Fk is a deduction. Since B1, ..., Bk' belong to {A0, ..., Ak-1}, it follows that A0 , ..., Ak-1 ⇒ Ak. By applying these facts inductively, we can prove the following:

Proposition on Generation and Causality
Let C be a collection of events, B be an event, and assume that B is generated by C. Then there exist events C1, ..., Cm in C such that C1, ..., Cm ⇒ B.

Let L be a collection of laws. If L has the following property called omnipotence, then we say that L is omnipotent:

The following two expressions are equivalent for each tuple (A1, ..., An, B) of events:

  1. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  2. B is generated by the union L∪{A1, ..., An}.

Here, {A1, ..., An} denotes the collection of A1, ..., An, and L∪{A1, ..., An} is the union of the collections L and {A1, ..., An}. It follow from Proposition on Generation and Causality that condition 2 implies condition 1. Therefore, the omnipotence of L, in fact, means that, for each tuple (A1, ..., An, B) of events, condition 1 implies condition 2. A similar remark applies to the following definition of omniscience.

If L has the following property called omniscience, then we say that L is omniscient:

The following two expressions are equivalent for each tuple (A1, ..., An, B) of events:

  1. B is an effect of the causes A1, ..., An.
  2. There exists an explanation of B by L∪{A1, ..., An}.

Then Proposition on Generation and Explanation shows that the following proposition holds:

Proposition on Omnipotence and Omniscience
Omnipotence and omniscience on a collection of laws are equivalent, that is, it is omnipotent if and only if it is omniscient.

If a collection of laws has these equivalent properties, we say that it governs causality.

1.5. Noological Set Theory

A clarification of my view on sets is in order, prompted by the following statement in §1.2: ''Answer to Question 2:''

In the mathematical science mentioned above, the noo-worlds are defined as sets, whereas the noo-cosmos cannot be defined as a set due to a certain set-theoretical reason to be clarified in §1.5: ''Noological Set Theory.''

The set theory presented here is neither naïve nor axiomatic, but rather noological.

The Noological Definition of Sets
A set is defined as a collection of entities — called the elements of the set — within a perceived range. These entities are gathered by our intellect, and the set itself is regarded as an entity.

This definition is noological because of the italicized words ''intellect,'' ''perceived,'' and ''entities,'' — an noo-element is called an entity if it makes sense to ask whether it exists.

Now, we can proceed as in naïve set theory. However, there exist certain differences. Notably, the following holds:

Proposition on Belonginess
Every set is distinct from its elements, that is, it does not belong to itself.

This is because each set is constructed by our intellect, and we cannot include the set among its elements prior to constructing it. This is analogous to the fact that we cannot include a dish among its ingredients prior to making it.

Entities other than sets are called prime entities. For example, the symbol I is a prime entity, as are all of its sequences such as I, II, III, and so on. We call the prime entities I, II, III, ... the natural numbers as the ancient romans probably did, and as usual, denote them respectively by 1, 2, 3, ... in the decimal system. Using them, we can construct the sets of integers, rational numbers, real numbers, and so on, in a well-known way. The numbers are all prime entities. Thus, our set theory is open in that it takes prime entities into consideration.

Proposition on Entitic Conditions (conditions on entities)
If an entitic condition C satisfies the following condition 1, then it must also satisfy the following condition 2:
  1. There exists the set of all entities satisfying C.
  2. For some set X, every set containing X does not satisfy C.
However, the converse is not always true, that is, there exists an entitic condition C which satisfies condition 2 but does not satisfy condition 1.
Proof of the first assertion
Suppose that an entitic condition C satisfies condition 1. Then there exists the set A of all entities satisfying C. By way of contradiction, assume that C does not satisfy condition 2. Then there exists a set B which contains A and satisfies C. The set B is an entity satisfying C, and so is an element of A. Since A is contained in B, B is an element B, contrary to Proposition on Belonginess.
Proof of the second assertion
By way of contradiction, assume that every entitic condition satisfying condition 2 also satisfies condition 1. Let p be a prime entity. Define the entitic condition Cp so that an entity x satisfies it if and only if x = p. Then every set does not satisfy Cp, and so Cp satisfies condition 2. Therefore, Cp satisfies condition 1 by the above assumption, that is, there exists the set {p} consisting only of p. Define the entitic condition C so that an entity x satisfies it if and only if x is a set whose elements are not prime entities. Then every set containing {p} does not satisfy C, and so C satisfies condition 2. Therefore, C satisfies condition 1 by the above assumption, that is, there exists the set A of all sets whose elements are not prime entities. But then, A is a set whose elements are not prime entities, and so A belongs to A, contrary to Proposition on Belonginess.

An entitic condition is said to be unbounded if it does not satisfy condition 2 in Proposition on Entitic Conditions. Namely, an entitic condition C is unbounded if, for each set X, there exists a set which contains X and satisfies C. Proposition on Entitic Conditions, then, shows that there does not exist the set of all entities satisfying C. This has various consequences about mathematics:

  1. There does not exist the set of all entities. This is because the empty entitic condition is obviously unbounded. Due to this, we cannot define noo-cosmos as a set, as was stated in §1.2: ''Answer to Question 2.''
  2. There does not exist the set of all sets. To see this, define the entitic condition C so that an entity x satisfies it if and only if x is a set. Then C is obviously unbounded. Due to this and Proposition on Belonginess, we can forget about the so-called Russel's paradox in naïve set theory. Also, we cannot develop the theory of cardinalities of sets as in naïve set theory, and similarly for order types.
  3. There does not exist the set of all well-ordered sets, and similarly for totally ordered sets and ordered sets. This is because every set is well-ordered by Zermelo's theorem.
  4. There does not exist the set of all groups, and similarly for monoids and semigroups. This is because, for an arbitrary given set X, there exists the free group whose basis is equal to X.
  5. For an arbitrarily given field K, there does not exist the set of all K-algebras, and similarly for K-vector spaces and rings. This is because every group G gives rise to a K-group algebra whose basis is equal to G.

1.6. Mistakes in Paradoxes

Since I mentioned Russel's paradox, some remarks on paradoxes may be in order. An event A is said to be paradoxical if both A and its negation -A are necessary, that is, if () ⇒ A and () ⇒ -A. In fact, no paradoxical event exists. This is verified by using the following logical laws:

  1. If A ⇒ B and B ⇒ C for events A, B, and C, then A ⇒ C.
  2. If -A ⇒ B for events A and B, then () ⇒ A OR B.
  3. If () ⇒ A OR -B for events A and B, then B ⇒ A.
  4. If () ⇒ A OR A for an event A, then () ⇒ A.
  5. -(-A) ⇒ A for each event A.

It is a good exercise to derive the following laws from the above ones:

  1. If -A ⇒ -B for events A and B, then B ⇒ A.
  2. If -A ⇒ A, then () ⇒ A.
  3. If A ⇒ -A, then () ⇒ -A.

Law 6 was used in the proof of Proposition on Entitic Conditions. The above-mentioned fact may be stated and proved as follows:

Proposition on Paradoxes
No paradoxical event exists.
Proof
Suppose A is a paradoxical event. Then () ⇒ A and () ⇒ -A, and so X ⇒ A and -Y ⇒ -A for arbitrary events X and Y; the latter yields A ⇒ Y by Law 6, hence X ⇒ Y by Law 1. However, for instance, ''x is x'' ⇒ -(''x is x'') does not hold for every entity x.

I define a paradox as a reasoning which concludes that there exists a paradoxical event, which is incorrect according to Proposition on Paradoxes. Incidentally, the prefix ''para-'' in particular means ''incorrect,'' and ''dox'' derives from ''dokein,'' meaning ''to think.''

Examples of Paradoxes and Their Mistakes

Example 1. Achilles does not catch up with the tortoise:
Achilles runs a race with a tortoise. The tortoise starts a meters ahead of him. Achilles and the tortoise run at b meters per second and c meters per second, respectively, with b greater than c. Then, the distance between them decreases at a rate of (b – c) meters per second, and becomes zero after a/(b – c) seconds, that is, Achilles catches up with the tortoise at that time. On the other hand, he reaches the tortoise's starting point after a/b seconds, by which time the tortoise has moved ac/b meters ahead. He then reaches that new point after ac/b2 seconds, by which time the tortoise has moved ac2/b2 meters further. He reaches that point after ac2/b3 seconds, when the tortoise is ac3/b3 meters ahead; and so on. Therefore, the tortoise remains ahead of Achilles, always, and he does not catch up with the tortoise.

This reasoning concludes that both the event ''Achilles catches up with the tortoise'' and its negation are necessary; hence, it is paradoxical. According to Proposition on Paradoxes, there must be a mistake here. The total time during which the tortoise remains ahead of Achilles is less than the infinite sum (a/b + ac/b2 + ac2/b3 + ・・・), which is equal to a/(b-c) seconds — the exact time at which Achilles catches up with the tortoise. Therefore, the description following ''On the other hand'' up to ''and so on'' at most means ''The tortoise remains ahead of Achilles, until he catches up with it,'' which is correct. The mistake lies in replacing ''until he catches up with it'' with ''always.''

Example 2. There is a declarative which is both false and not false:
Consider the declarative ''This declarative is false.'' If this declarative is false, then it is not false. If it is not false, then it is false.

Let A denote the event ''This declarative is not false.'' Then this reasoning concludes that -A ⇒ A and A ⇒ -A. Hence it is paradoxical by the laws 7 and 8 above. According to Proposition on Paradoxes, there must be a mistake here. Let B denote the first underlined ''this declarative.'' Then the second underlined ''this declarative'' refers to the declarative ''B is false.'' Let C denote it. Then the above reasoning in fact concludes that B is false if and only if C is not false, which is correct. Hence B is not equal to C. Probably, misled by the ambiguity of the word ''this declarative,'' the above reasoning assumed that B was equal to C, hence concluded that B and C were both false and not false.

Example 3. Inexpressible entities are expressible:
Let a denote one of the entities which are inexpressible. Then a is expressible as ''one of the entities which are inexpressible,'' and so a is not one of the entities which are inexpressible. Thus, a is not a.

This reasoning concludes that the event ''a is not a'' is necessary, hence paradoxical, because its negation ''a is a'' is also necessary. According to Proposition on Paradoxes, there must be a mistake here. In fact, after expressing an entity as ''an inexpressible entity,'' it is lying as ''it is an inexpressible entity.'' There is another mistake. Every entity is expressible, even by one letter, as has been shown by the above sentence ''Let a denote one of the entities which are inexpressible.'' Similar remarks apply to the paradoxes obtained by replacing ''express'' by ''explain'' and ''define.''

If x is an entity and A is a set, the expression ''x ∈ A'' means ''x belongs to A.'' Russel's paradox in naïve set theory is as follows:

Let A be the set of all sets X which do not satisfy X ∈ X. Then, if A satisfies A ∈ A, then A does not satisfy A ∈ A by the definition of A. If A does not satisfy A ∈ A, then A satisfies A ∈ A by the definition of A.

Let B denote the event ''A does not satisfy A ∈ A.'' Then Russel concluded above that -B ⇒ B and B ⇒ -B. Hence B is paradoxical by the laws 7 and 8 above. According to Proposition on Paradoxes, there must be a mistake here. As shown above, it was found out by proceeding to noological set theory, which is open and broad. It is a pity that the initiators of naïve set theory could not find out it, and some of them proceeded to axiomatic set theory, which is closed and narrow.

2.3. The Self, Values, Emotion, Volition, and Memory

Although the IS is originally the intellection system, we may also consider the systems of values, emotion, volition, and memory grounded in IS.

Let p and q be evential percepts. A causal connection of p with q is defined as a sequence a0, ..., an of evential percepts, where n is a nonnegative integer called the length of the connection, such that an = q and, for each integer k from 0 through n, ak satisfies one of the following two conditions:

  1. ak is either equal to p or a percept of a law.
  2. ak belongs to fk({a0, ..., ak-1}) for a deductive operation fk of the RS.

Here, {a0, ..., ak-1} denotes the collection of a0, ..., ak-1 and the meaning of fk({a0, ..., ak-1}) is explained in the remark below.If a causal connection of p with q exists, then we say that p is causally connected with q and let D(p,q) denote the minimal length of all such connections. Clearly, D(p,q) = 0 if and only if p = q. The causal graph is then defined as the oriented graph whose nodes are the evential percepts and whose arrows are the causal connections.

Let P, Q, A0, ..., An respectively denote the objects of the evential percepts p, q, a0, ..., an in the above definition of a causal connection. They are events. Since an = q, it follows that An = Q. Suppose ak satisfies condition 2 above. Then, by definition, there exists a tuple (b1, ..., bk') of evential percepts in {a0, ..., ak-1} such that ak = fk(b1, ..., bk'). Since fk is a deductive operation, the objects B1, ..., Bk' of b1, ..., bk' usually satisfy B1, ..., Bk' ⇒ Ak. If they satisfy that, then since B1, ..., Bk' belong to {A0, ..., Ak-1}, we conclude that A0, ..., Ak-1 ⇒ Ak. If ak satisfies condition 1 above, then Ak is either equal to P or a law. By applying these facts inductively, we can show that usually P ⇒ Ak for each k, and in particular usually P ⇒ Q. Thus, the causal connection of p with q may be regarded as an internal explanation of why usually P ⇒ Q.

As defined in §0.3: ''Causality and Necessity,'' an event is said to be contingent if it is neither necessary nor impossible, that is, if it is unnecessary and possible. Accordingly, an evential percept is said to be contingent if its object is contingent. The above remark shows that if p is a contingent evential percept, then, usually, p is not causally connected with some evential percept, and some evential percept is not causally connected with p.

Hypotheses on Values, Emotion, Volition, and Memory
Hypothesis 1. There exists a value origin percept o, that is, a percept of a contingent event called the value origin event.
The assumption that the value origin event is contingent is not theoretically necessary. It is practically essential, because the most fundamental value origin event is the event ''my life continues,'' where life is defined as in Observation 5, and this event appears to be contingent.
Hypothesis 2. If an evential percept p is causally connected with the value origin percept o, then the anti-value of p is defined as D(p,o).
Hypothesis 3. Emotion for an event is determined by the anti-values of its evential percepts.
Hypothesis 4. Whether an act becomes a will and the strength of that will are determined by the anti-values of the evential percepts of the act.

3.2. Various Aspects of Life

A life system, being a system, necessarily possesses a clear boundary between its interior and exterior. Since its purpose is the continuation of its life — that is, the persistence of its autonomous self-reproduction process — it must maintain within itself a blueprint and a procedural scheme for self-reproduction. It must also, as needed, acquire materials and sources of energy from its surroundings, store them internally, and discharge surplus substances back into the environment.

These acts of intake, storage, and discharge are all subject, to varying degrees, to the conditions of both the external and internal environments. Consequently, even though the self-reproduction of a life system is autonomous and directed toward its own continuation, it is neither entirely free nor constant. It is constrained to some extent by those conditions, and when the constraints are severe, the process may cease altogether. When the constraints are mild, however, the process can continue, adapting to the conditions as necessary. It should be noted that the external environment of one life system may include the self-reproduction activities of other life systems.

The replicas produced by a life system — whether of itself or of its components — may emerge either within or outside the system. Even if initially produced internally, they may eventually be released into the external environment.

Moreover, these replicas are not necessarily exact copies; they may vary in various ways. Even when they are accurate copies, they may undergo changes after production.

Moreover, such replicas may either be produced as life systems themselves or become life systems after their production. In either case, the original life system is called the parent, and the resulting life system is called the offspring. The offspring of the offspring, and so on, are called descendants.

Strictly speaking, an offspring is distinct from its parent, but it may also be regarded as continuous with the parent. If so regarded, each individual life continues until its descendants come to an end. This continuity may be likened to a factory that, even after being rebuilt multiple times, is still considered the same factory as long as the same production process continues. This remark leads to my view of life and death, to be presented in §3.3: ''Baton of Life.''

Since self-reproduction is constrained by internal and external conditions, it is more readily achieved in environments where such constraints are minimal. Moreover, as noted above, self-reproduction inevitably involves variation. Depending on the attributes resulting from such variation, the degree of constraint imposed by the environment may change. Thus, as the production of offspring continues — offspring of offspring, and so on — groups of life systems possessing particular attributes may gradually increase or decrease in number depending on their compatibility with internal and external conditions. This principle may be called the principle of gradual change, as it gradually gives rise to various phenomena, including those to be discussed in the succeeding paragraphs.

As stated in §3.1: ''Systems with Life and Survival Instinct,'' a life system is a system endowed with a purpose of survival. 3.3. Baton of Life

People die in many different ways. If we were to regard all forms of death as misfortunes, then — since everyone must eventually die — it would follow that every human being, including the newborn child whose birth is now being celebrated, is destined to end in misfortune. I do not wish to hold such a view of life and death.

Biology suggests a view of life and death that stands apart — not only from the notion that all death is misfortune, but also from religious and ancient views. The reproductive cells produced by your body, when united with those of another, can undergo repeated cell division and develop into a new human being. While the other cells in your body will eventually age and die, your reproductive cells may live on as a new person who inherits your genetic traits. If health permits and no other events intervene, this process can continue unbroken from generation to generation. In this sense, every human being is originally endowed with eternal life.

To put it metaphorically, we may think of life as batons. Each individual life is like a baton in a relay race — one without a finish line, without winners or losers. Each of us is entrusted with the vital role of receiving the baton and passing it on, as a member of a team. If one can leave the stage with the peace of mind that one has fulfilled this role, then such a departure can hardly be called unfortunate. Moreover, even after fulfilling this role, one does not usually exit immediately; rather, one often remains to witness the baton being passed on, or even to assist in the passing. This, too, may be seen as a reward for having fulfilled one's role. Or perhaps it is a mistaken sense of completion, and in truth, we are often being urged to pass on many more batons.

These urgings, these rewards, and the very role of passing on the baton of life — all of them are granted to us by the principle of life described in §3.1: ''Systems with Life and Survival Instinct.'' It is this principle that allows even the most innocent of girls to grow into a child, then a maiden, fall in love, become a mother, a grandmother, and eventually an ancestor with countless descendants. The same is true for boys. In this way, the baton of life continues to be passed on.

In our modern, scientifically advanced age, I believe that spreading this biologically grounded view of life and death is a sound and compassionate way to help people overcome fear and sorrow in the face of death — both their own and that of others. Moreover, such a view brings with it a beautiful byproduct: a deepening sense of affection and compassion for one's parents and children, and by extension, for one's ancestors and descendants. For your ancestors and descendants are, in a profound sense, your past and future selves. Love for one's descendants naturally leads to the desire to contribute to the survival of humankind — for it is only through the continued existence of humanity that your descendants can live.

This desire lies at the very foundation of Eusophy proposed in ''FE'' and developed throughout this article, as well as of the answer to the Universal Question: ''What should anyone do?'' Such a view of life and death may also inspire children to embrace the goal of becoming able to pass on the baton of life. And for children who suffer so deeply — perhaps due to bullying at school — that they feel driven to end their lives, this view may offer a reason to live: to stay alive until they can pass on the baton they have received. For to pass on the baton of life is to meet one's future self in the next generation — a thrilling and hopeful prospect.

Incidentally, in Mozart's opera Die Zauberflöte (The Magic Flute), there is a joyful duet, Pa-Pa-Pa-Papageno, in which a loving couple sings of their future together and the many children they hope to have. Just before this scene, the man had been on the verge of taking his own life. This has led to the popularization of the Papageno effect as a means of suicide prevention in adults. Yet this song may also have a powerful message for children: it tells them that their parents are overjoyed to have become their parents, and it may awaken in them the aspiration to become someone who can pass on the baton of life. In this way, it may help children find happiness and hope.

3.4. Species

Answering the question ''What is a species?'' appears to be a difficult task in taxonomy, especially when it comes to classifying extinct organisms known only from fossils. However, Eusophy is oriented toward the future — specifically, toward the survival of humankind as a species. Accordingly, in this article, when I refer to humankind or living beings, I generally mean future humankind or future living beings. Since the present and even the recent past were, not long ago, part of the future, the term ''future'' effectively includes the present and the near past as well.

When it comes to future living beings, the question ''What is a species?'' is not so difficult to answer. I define two individuals as belonging to the same species if they are capable of continuous interbreeding — that is, if they can mate and produce offspring that can, in turn, mate with the original individuals. This is not merely a definition; if it becomes necessary to determine whether two individuals belong to the same species, the determination should be made according to this definition.

Such determination does not require direct mating between the individuals in question or between parents and offspring. It suffices to test mating compatibility with preserved specimens. For this purpose, if feasible, we should freeze and store multiple samples of eggs and sperm from various organisms, updating the specimens periodically.

To illustrate: although humanity comprises many races, the history of intermarriage among them has demonstrated that humans are capable of continuous interbreeding. This confirms Declaration (6): ''Humankind is but one among many species.'' In fact, we must define the scope of humankind itself. I propose that any individual capable of continuous interbreeding with human specimens be defined as belonging to humankind. This definition alone suffices; it is not necessary to determine, for every individual, whether it belongs to humankind.

Horses and donkeys can interbreed, but their offspring are sterile and thus not capable of continuous interbreeding. Therefore, they are distinct species. In contrast, pigs and wild boars are likely the same species, since pigs are domesticated boars. Similarly, dogs and wolves may also be the same species, given their shared ancestry and interbreeding potential.

To divide organisms into species based on continuous interbreeding, the transitivity law must hold: if individual A can continuously interbreed with individual B, and B with individual C, then A must also be able to continuously interbreed with C. This law is both necessary and sufficient for such a classification.

If the transitivity law fails, or if continuous interbreeding cannot be determined in principle, or if the organisms in question do not reproduce through mating at all, then — if classification remains important — it should be carried out using alternative criteria appropriate to the reasons for its importance. The same applies to organisms such as cultivated plants and domesticated animals, which have undergone extensive artificial breeding and for which classification by continuous interbreeding may not be appropriate.

There is little need to be overly concerned about how to classify species, because the only taxonomically important fact in this article is the above-mentioned Declaration (6), and the manner in which species are classified does not affect the explanation of evolution in the next subsection.

3.5. Evolution

In this section, I do not aim to define the standard meanings of the major terms in evolutionary theory, but rather to present my own interpretation. Let us begin by stating what I shall call the principle of evolution — also referred to as the principle of gradual change, as mentioned in §3.2: ''Various Aspects of Life:''

Within a single species sharing a common habitat, if individuals possessing a certain genetic trait leave more offspring than those lacking it, then the proportion of individuals with that trait will, over successive generations, approach 100%.

The conclusion of the principle of evolution — that the proportion of individuals possessing a certain genetic trait approaches 100% over successive generations — does not necessarily mean that the proportion will reach 100%. As the proportion of individuals with the trait increases, the habitat may change — for example, through pollution, depletion of food sources, or loss of suitable living space. In such cases, individuals with the trait may no longer leave significantly more offspring than those without it. As a result, the proportion of individuals with the trait may stabilize at, say, 70%, or even begin to decline.

Assume as in the principle of evolution that individuals possessing a certain genetic trait leave more offspring than those lacking it. Then we may say that the trait — or the individuals possessing it — is adapted to the habitat, or that it represents the fittest in that environment. If the habitat is subject to human influence, as in the case of cultivated fields or breeding facilities — or even if not — we may personify the environment and say that the trait or individuals have been selected by the environment. We may also describe the outcome of the principle by saying that the trait or the individuals possessing it tend toward plenitude — that is, toward being fully present throughout the habitat. Thus, the principle may be restated in various ways:

  • Within a single species sharing a common habitat, genetic traits or individuals adapted to that environment tend toward plenitude.
  • Within a single species sharing a common habitat, the fittest tend toward plenitude.
  • Within a single species sharing a common habitat, genetic traits or individuals selected by the environment tend toward plenitude.

In the Book of Genesis in the Old Testament, there is a well-known story of Noah's Ark. According to the narrative, when evil had spread throughout human society, God regretted having created humankind. He instructed Noah, a righteous man, to build an ark, and then sent a great flood to destroy all humans except Noah and his family. After the flood, God once again spoke the words He had originally addressed to the first humans: ''Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth.''

Strikingly, this episode can be interpreted as a parable that illustrates the principle of evolution described above. That is, the individuals (Noah and his descendants) who were selected by the environment (represented by God) go on to tend toward plenitude — they are fruitful, they multiply, and they fill the earth. This interpretation is by no means far-fetched. The Book of Genesis expresses the worldview and understanding of life held by ancient peoples, shaped in a time when civilization — understood, as in Observation 6, as the totality of human knowledge — was still in its infancy. If we read such texts as parables and seek their underlying meanings, we may find that they resonate with modern science and can even be harmonized with a scientific worldview and understanding of life.

Let us suppose that, by some principle distinct from the principle of evolution, a single species of life first emerged in the primordial world. On that basis, I propose the following as the law of evolution, and assume it to be valid:

Through variation during autonomous genetic self-reproduction, plenitude, migration, and repeated dispersion and aggregation, various species have emerged, undergone changes in their genetic traits, and either survived (as species) or failed to survive.

Moreover, I shall refer to the changes in genetic traits of a species — caused by variation during autonomous genetic self-reproduction, plenitude, migration, and repeated dispersion and aggregation — as the evolution of that species or of those traits. In this sense, evolution refers to changes that occur over successive generations, and in most species, such changes are gradual.

The global spread of the novel coronavirus that began in 2020 provides empirical support for the validity of this law of evolution. The virus that causes COVID-19 undergoes various mutations as it replicates within the human body. Through transmission from person to person, it migrates and undergoes repeated dispersion and aggregation. Variants with higher infectivity — those that leave more offspring — are the fittest within the habitat of the human body and tend toward plenitude. In this way, they survive.

As stated in §3.1: ''Systems with Life and Survival Instinct,'' viruses are life systems within the human body. That the law of evolution applies to such viruses is one piece of empirical evidence supporting its validity.

3.6. The Biosphere for Humankind

The aim of Eusophy — to explore the path to the survival of humankind — does not imply that the survival of other species is to be disregarded. Of course, there are species such as Mycobacterium tuberculosis whose survival we may justifiably seek to eliminate. However, the survival of humankind depends on the survival of many other species. The reasons for this are somewhat intricate.

Let us begin by imagining a time before the advent of agriculture, animal husbandry, or aquaculture, when humans obtained food through hunting, gathering, and fishing. In those times, humans survived by consuming various wild organisms, each of which, in turn, had survived by consuming other organisms. In this way, humans lived within a food chain — a chain of eating and being eaten. This food chain is itself long, but it also connects to even longer chains. The remains of organisms consumed and excreted by animals are further consumed and excreted by other animals, or ultimately decomposed by microorganisms into soil components, or transformed into atmospheric CO2 (carbon dioxide) through combustion or slow oxidation. Over long periods, these remains may also become carbon compounds in the earth or sea, eventually returning to the atmosphere as CO2.

Yet even this is not the end. The remains that have become soil components or atmospheric CO2 are absorbed by plants — through their roots or via photosynthesis — and become part of plant bodies. These plants are then consumed by animals, and the cycle continues. This broader network of relationships, which includes but extends beyond the food chain, forms a system in which ''consuming and being consumed'' occurs in a wide sense, and in which matter circulates and returns to its origin. Rather than the commonly used term ''material cycle,'' I shall refer to this as the food cycle.

Even today, when most of our food comes from agriculture, animal husbandry, and aquaculture, the food cycle continues. While hunting and gathering have diminished, fishing has expanded. All living organisms, including humans, continue to survive within the food cycle. In Observation 7, I provisionally defined the food cycle as the composite of the food chain and the carbon cycle, where the latter refers to the circulation of carbon among the atmosphere, the earth's crust, the oceans, and living organisms through various carbon compounds.

The relationships among organisms that support survival are not limited to the food cycle. Many organisms survive through symbiosis — mutual support among different species. It is now well known that animals live in symbiosis with bacteria in their bodies. Plants also engage in symbiosis — with fungi, with other plants, and with soil organisms through networks of roots and mycelia. When insects or birds feed on nectar or pollen and assist in pollination, this too is a form of symbiosis. Insects that damage plants may be eaten by birds, or seeds consumed by birds may be dispersed and deposited elsewhere, allowing plants to expand their habitat — these are also forms of symbiosis. Even human cultivation of plants and domestication of animals can be seen as symbiosis: those plants and animals survive by being utilized by humans.

Biologists believe that organelles such as mitochondria and chloroplasts, which support the survival of many organisms, originated from bacteria that once lived symbiotically within primitive cells. These are examples of interspecies symbiosis, but intraspecies symbiosis also exists. Social insects such as ants and bees, and other social organisms that live in structured communities, can be seen as engaging in intraspecies symbiosis. So too can sexually reproducing organisms, in which males and females of the same species coexist and cooperate. In fact, multicellular organisms themselves may be viewed as symbiotic collectives of single-celled organisms of the same kind. Their reproductive cells correspond to the females or queens in social species. Or rather, we may imagine that primitive multicellular organisms, in seeking greater efficiency in survival, ceased expanding as single bodies and instead evolved into social or sexually reproducing organisms. The size and structural complexity of such organisms may have evolved to the extent that maximizes survival efficiency.

The relationships among organisms that support survival are not limited to the food cycle and symbiosis. In many cases, the components of an organism's habitat are provided by other organisms. I shall refer to this relationship as habitat bioprovision. For example, forests, grasslands, seagrass beds, and coral reefs — key components of many animals' habitats — are created by plants or other animals. The soil that supports plant life is formed from organic remains by insects, fungi, and microorganisms. In parasitism or infection, the body of one organism becomes the habitat of another, often without mutual benefit — unlike symbiosis. What, then, constitutes the habitat of humankind? Even a person living in a high-rise apartment in a concrete jungle depends on food, clothing, and shelter made from materials produced around the world. Their habitat thus includes the habitats of those who produce such materials — farmlands, pastures, fisheries, forests — all of which are shaped and sustained by living organisms. The oxygen that sustains nearly all life today was first produced by cyanobacteria in ancient times and continues to be generated by plants.

From these examples alone, it is clear that the survival of any species depends on the survival of many other species and the sustainability of their habitats, through the food cycle, symbiosis, and habitat bioprovision. The biosphere is the system composed of all biological communities and their habitats. I shall refer to the network of connections described above as the biospherical network:

Biospherical network: the network of food cycles, symbioses, and habitat bioprovisions that link various biological communities and their habitats within the biosphere.

Thus, the biosphere is a system in which biological communities and their habitats are interconnected throughout the biospherical network. The survival of any species depends on the survival of many other species and the sustainability of their habitats, all linked throughout this network. And as stated in Declaration (6), humankind is one among many species and a member of the biosphere, as the title ''Humankind as Part of the Biosphere'' of this section suggests. Therefore, I go one step further than the earlier claim that ''the survival of humankind depends on the survival of many other species,'' and assert the following:

The survival of humankind depends on the sustainability of the biosphere.

However, the biosphere is dynamic and has reached its current state through a long process of transformation, and this process will likely continue. What matters is that this transformation proceeds in a direction favorable to humankind — that is, that the biosphere does not evolve into a state that makes human survival more difficult. Ideally, this favorable transformation should continue for as long as possible — perhaps for billions of years, as long as solar energy remains available.

As noted earlier, there may be species such as Mycobacterium tuberculosis whose extinction is acceptable. Therefore, the ''sustainability of the biosphere'' mentioned above does not mean preserving the biosphere in its current form. Rather, it means ensuring that its ongoing transformation continues in a direction that is as favorable as possible to human survival. This is what I meant by the title ''The Biosphere for Humankind'' of this subsection.

4.1. Causality in Science

It is a well-known story: in the seventeenth century, Newton's theory of mechanics resolved seemingly unrelated questions — such as ''Why does an apple fall from a tree?'' and ''Why do planets orbit the sun?'' — by appealing to the law of universal gravitation and the laws of motion, which describe how forces acting on a body affect its motion. Similarly, observation shows that the event ''X is a planet'' is causally connected with the event ''X orbits the sun.''Indeed, the celestial bodies that once appeared to wander across the sky — hence the name ''planet,'' derived from the Greek ''planētēs,'' meaning ''wanderer'' — were found by Kepler and others to orbit the sun. Newton mathematically proved that these empirically observed causal connections are governed by the law of universal gravitation and the laws of motion. That is, he demonstrated that one aspect of causality — the motion of physical bodies — is governed by these laws.

Generalizing from this, we may say, as above, that science is the totality of endeavors to discover, with evidence, the laws that govern aspects of causality. This is why science is said to be universal. It is also why science is considered objective — and why I believe that scientists are among the most sincere and trustworthy individuals when it comes to expressing knowledge: they pursue evidence.

Evidence, however, is not limited to mathematical proof. It also includes empirical methods such as experimentation and observation. Even mathematical proofs rely on mathematical models — as discussed in §1.7 — which are themselves based on empirical methods. In this sense, mathematical proof is also a kind of empirical evidence. Therefore, even laws once believed to be correct may be revised when experimental or observational techniques improve. Indeed, Newton's theory of mechanics was revised 240 years later by Einstein's theory of relativity.

Some may criticize science for being subject to revision and conclude that scientists are untrustworthy. But it is precisely because scientists continually pursue the best available evidence that such revisions occur. For this reason, I maintain that scientists are among the most sincere and trustworthy individuals in expressing what they know.

Newtonian mechanics is also a prime example of the mathematical sciences described in §1.7. As I have noted on various occasions, the contents of ''MP,'' ''MN,'' and ''FE'' likewise belong to the domain of mathematical science. So too does Mendelian genetics, as mentioned in §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language.''

Science, understood as an intellectual endeavor to discover — with evidence — laws that govern aspects of causality, is not confined to laboratories. In fact, it is something that everyone engages in, often unconsciously, in the course of daily life. For example, in relation to food, clothing, and shelter — aspects of causality that concern basic human needs — anyone might discover, through empirical evidence, laws such as: ''If I overeat, I feel uncomfortably full,'' ''If I wear many layers, I stay warm,'' or ''If I stay indoors, I feel relaxed.'' These are, in a sense, scientific insights.

However, this kind of science might be more appropriately called amateur science. In this article, unless otherwise specified, the term ''science'' refers to the kind conducted in laboratories, and ''scientists'' are those who engage in such science. Statements such as ''Modern scientific knowledge is generally accurate and advanced'' do not refer to amateur science. Amateur science is often neither advanced nor accurate, and it is not uncommon for it to contain serious errors.

I once heard a story about a certain premodern society. There, people had noticed that when lice fled from a sick person's body, death often followed soon after. They came to fear that ''people die because the lice leave,'' and so they would deliberately place lice on their bodies.Of course, the truth is the reverse: ''Lice leave because the person is dying.'' Not only had they confused cause and effect, but they were also unaware of a more serious fact — that being bitten by lice increases the risk of infectious disease.

We should not be too quick to laugh at such societies. In our own, for instance, some people learn that ''many individuals with blood type A tend to be cautious'' and conclude, ''So being type A makes one more cautious.'' Others reject this, saying, ''Blood type couldn't possibly influence personality.'' Both positions make the same mistake: they oversimplify the relationship between cause and effect. The truth may be something like this: ''People with blood type A tend to have more biological vulnerabilities, and over the course of evolution, those who were more cautious were better adapted to their environments and left more offspring.'' If so, this would be a significant insight — especially for those with blood type A.

It is worth noting that, just as there is amateur science, there may also be amateur scholarship. For example, the term ''philosophy'' in everyday language often refers not to academic philosophy, but to fragments, remnants, or faint wisps of amateur thought. In this article, however, amateur scholarship other than amateur science lies outside our scope. Thus, unless otherwise specified, suffixes like ''ology'' or ''ologist'' refer to academic disciplines and those who engage in them within research institutions.

4.2. Causality in Logic

Logic is the totality of endeavors to understand causality on the basis of the laws that govern the assumption–consequence relation among declaratives

Let us now consider, in light of the previous subsection §4.1: ''Causality in Science,'' the following formulation: Let A denote the event ''X is Y,'' B denote the event ''X does Z,'' and C denote the event ''All Y do Z.''

In §4.1, Y referred to specific entities, such as ''apples that have detached from a branch'' or ''planets,'' and Z referred to specific acts, such as ''fall'' or ''orbit the sun.'' Accordingly, C represented specific events such as ''All apples that have detached from a branch (do) fall'' or ''All planets (do) orbit the sun.''

In standard logic textbooks, one often encounters the example where X, Y, and Z are ''Socrates,'' ''a human,'' and ''be mortal,'' respectively. In that case, A, C, and B correspond to the events ''Socrates is a human,'' ''All humans are mortal,'' and ''Socrates is mortal.''

Now, we all know that — regardless of what X, Y, and Z may be — if both A and C occur, then B occurs. That is, the events A and C together are causally connected with B, as defined in §0.3: ''Causality and Necessity.''

As we grow intellectually, we humans naturally acquire the ability to connect (A, C) with B, and to perform similar operations. However, in daily life, we do so without conscious awareness, and we rarely distinguish among the various forms these operations may take.

Logicians, by contrast, have identified and distinguished such operations, discovering laws like the syllogism — which asserts that, for any X, Y, and Z, the pair (A, C) is causally connected with B. Indeed, it was likely such discoveries by ancient philosophers that marked the beginning of logic as a discipline.

The syllogism is implicitly at work in §4.1 as well. What Newton did, in effect, was to prove — using mathematics and the laws of universal gravitation and motion — that C necessarily occurs for Y and Z specified as above. Combining this with the syllogism, we can conclude that, for any X, A is causally connected with B for those specific Y and Z.

In fact, there are other logical laws also referred to as syllogisms. In ''MN,'' the validity of the syllogism described above is explained on the basis of case logic — a framework developed therein.

As noted above, we used A to denote the event ''X is Y.'' Strictly speaking, however, ''X is Y'' is the linguistic expression of event A. The same applies to the relationship between ''X does Z'' and B, and between ''All Y do Z'' and C. Thus, X and Y represent general linguistic expressions of entities, and Z represents a general linguistic expression of an act of an entity.

As explained in §0.2: ''Intellect and the Objective World,'' linguistic expressions of entities and events are called nominals and declaratives, respectively. Linguistic expressions of acts are called predicates — since acts are a kind of event, predicates are a kind of declarative expression.

Accordingly, the syllogism is, in essence, a law concerning declaratives. It states that, for any nominals X and Y and any predicate Z, if the declaratives ''X is Y'' and ''All Y do Z'' are both true, then the declarative ''X does Z'' is also true.

As written in §0.3, when the occurrence of certain events leads to the occurrence of other events, we call the former the causes and the latter the effects. Similarly, when the truth of certain declaratives leads to the truth of other declaratives, we may call the former the assumptions and the latter the consequences.

Thus, for any nominals X and Y and any predicate Z, the syllogism connects the pair of declaratives (''X is Y,'' ''All Y do Z'') with the declarative ''X does Z'' in an assumption–consequence relationship. This corresponds to the causal connection between the pair of events (A, C) and the event B.

Generalizing from this, we may say that modern logic is the study of the laws that govern assumption–consequence relationships among declaratives. To discover such laws is to discover the laws that govern causality across the diverse universes — the noo-worlds — that constitute the multiverse of the noo-cosmos.

However, in this article, I use the term ''logic'' to refer more broadly to any intellectual endeavor that seeks to understand causality on the basis of logical laws. The adjective ''logical'' is derived from this broader sense of ''logic.''

Taken together with the previous subsection §4.1, which defined science as ''an intellectual endeavor to discover, with evidence, laws that govern aspects of causality,'' we can see that modern science and logic share a common feature: they both aim to understand causality through evidential laws.

This view of logic is well suited to the concerns and methods of AI engineering, which asks: ''How can we enable machines to judge — or be made to judge — causality mechanically?'' Of course, if the concerns or methods differ, other perspectives may be more appropriate. For example, in the noology of ''MN,'' logic is used in order to construct and analyze a mathematical model of the noo-trinity described in §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language.''

4.3. Causality in Ethics

Ethics is the speculative study and teaching of actions that are causally connected with being morally good

Confucianism is often regarded as a religion, but it is more accurately understood as a form of ethics. One of its principal texts, the ''Analects,'' is a record of the sayings and actions of Confucius, the founder of Confucianism.

Analects 14:25 (Xian Wen 25) is a brief passage: ''The Master said: In ancient times, those who studied did so for themselves; today, they study for others.'' Setting aside the opening phrase ''The Master said,'' the standard interpretation is that this is a teaching of social morality: ''If you wish to be morally good, study not to gain recognition from others, but to cultivate yourself.'' Here, ''those who study'' likely refers to all who engage in learning, including students. A similar interpretation would be: ''If you wish to be morally good, study not to surpass others, but to improve yourself.'' This is because, as the following examples suggest, being recognized by others often amounts to defeating one's competitors.

Analects 2:3 (Wei Zheng 3) is another brief passage: ''The Master said: Guide the people with laws and regulate them with punishments, and they will avoid wrongdoing but lack a sense of shame. Guide them with virtue and regulate them with ritual, and they will develop a sense of shame and become upright.'' This is a teaching of political morality: ''If you wish to promote moral conduct, guide the people not with laws and punishments, but with personal virtue and propriety.''

Generalizing from these examples, Confucianism may be described as a system of teachings on morally appropriate conduct in society and politics. And since ethics is the study and teaching of morally appropriate conduct, I have written that Confucianism is more accurately regarded as ethics.

To be regarded as a form of ethics is, in fact, an honor for Confucianism. The reason becomes clear in the next subsection (§4.4), where religion is compared with science, logic, and ethics. Science, logic, and ethics possess no inherently undesirable features, whereas religion has many.

Of course, it is not easy to practice the teachings of Analects 2:3 or even 14:25 in today's world. For students, studying in order to gain admission to schools or secure employment — that is, to be recognized by examiners by outperforming competitors — is a pressing concern. Even university professors often study in order to retain their positions or secure research funding — that is, to gain recognition from superiors, colleagues, or society by surpassing their peers.

According to psychologists, people are most pleased when they are recognized by others. Likely, they are equally pleased when they surpass others. We can observe this not only through psychological experiments, but also in our daily interactions and social observations. Ultimately, as explained in §3.1: ''Systems with Life and Survival Instinct,'' this tendency likely stems from the biological imperative of sexually reproducing organisms — including humans — to defeat same-sex rivals and gain recognition and acceptance from the opposite sex in order to leave offspring. In other words, the joy of being recognized by others is rooted in the biological instinct for survival.

To sublimate such deeply rooted tendencies, we might adopt the following perspective: Let us humbly and critically consider whether we and others are truly worthy of recognition — both of being recognized and of recognizing. Suppose that we are indeed worthy of recognition, and that we are recognized by someone else who is also worthy of recognition. Would that bring us joy as scholars? I think not. For to be recognized by another means only that we remain within the bounds of that person's understanding — we have not yet reached a higher summit beyond their view.

To use a metaphor: The height of Mount Fuji is evident even from its base, but the appearance of its summit cannot be discerned even from the peaks of neighboring mountains. We should aim to become such a lofty mountain. Such a goal may transform the joy of being recognized or surpassing others into the joy of self-realization. For Mount Fuji, the surrounding mountains and the lowlands — even if not submerged in clouds — are as good as nonexistent. This, I believe, is the true spirit of Analects 14:25.

If we restate the standard interpretation of Analects 14:25 in more logical terms, it becomes: ''To be morally good, study not to gain recognition from others, but to cultivate yourself.'' Although the meaning of ''to be morally good'' is not entirely clear, this statement asserts that the act of studying for self-cultivation — rather than for recognition — is causally connected with being morally good.

Similarly, Analects 2:3 asserts that the act of guiding the people with virtue and propriety — rather than with laws and punishments — is causally connected with being morally good.

Generalizing from these examples, we may say that ethics is the study and teaching of actions that are causally connected with being morally good. Such actions are what I referred to earlier as ''morally appropriate conduct.''

However, the meaning of ''morally good'' is open to various interpretations. Accordingly, there are many kinds of ethics — such as social ethics and political ethics. In fact, the meaning of ''morally good'' in Analects 14:25 and in 2:3 is unclear, and they do not appear to mean the same thing.

Thus, teachers of ethics sometimes leave the meaning of ''morally good'' undefined. Even when they do define it, they often do not attempt to provide evidence of a causal connection between various actions and moral goodness. Unlike science and logic — which, as explained in §4.1 and §4.2, share the feature of understanding causality through evidential laws — teachers of ethics may assume the existence of deep, unverifiable connections and teach accordingly. As a result, students of ethics are left to speculate for themselves the meaning of ''morally good'' and the evidence — if any — for the causal connection between actions and moral goodness.

In fact, the literal meaning of the key phrase in Analects 14:25 — ''In ancient times, those who studied did so for themselves; today, they study for others'' — suggests a more straightforward interpretation: ''In the past, scholars studied for selfish reasons, while today they study to benefit others — and this is morally good.'' That is, ''If you wish to be morally good, study not for selfish gain, but to benefit others.'' To arrive at the standard interpretation mentioned earlier, one must make several assumptions: that Confucius regarded the actions of ancient scholars as morally good; that ''ancient scholars'' refers to Confucius himself; that ''for oneself'' means ''to cultivate oneself''; and that ''for others'' means ''to gain recognition from others.'' Such an interpretation requires multiple layers of speculation beyond the literal meaning.

In the end, ethics is a field of study based on speculations about actions that are causally connected with being morally good — that is, actions whose occurrence implies moral goodness, whatever that may mean. In this sense, ''being morally good'' functions as a value origin in the sense of §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language,'' and may thus be called the ethical origin.

Morally good actions are also called good deeds. Therefore, ''morally good'' may be rendered simply as ''good,'' and judgments of good and evil presuppose some standard.

Since good deeds may vary in degree, ethics itself may also vary in degree. There is, then, a highest good — the supreme good — and a corresponding supreme form of ethics. I have discussed what this supreme good and supreme ethics might be in Observation 8 and in §0.6: ''Conclusions on Eusophy and the Universal Question.''

4.4. Causality in Religion

Religion is, in essence, a system of speculation and prayer concerning causality and the acceptance of their outcomes

The term ''religion'' encompasses a vast diversity of traditions across the world. Even a single religion, identified by one name, often undergoes historical transformations and splinters into numerous sects. It is neither possible nor necessary to consider them all here. Thus, in this section, ''religion'' refers only to a few traditions I have personally encountered. The same limitation applies to science, logic, and ethics, though I have not previously stated it.

In Japan, religion typically refers to Shinto and Buddhism. As noted in the previous subsection, Confucianism is better understood as ethics than as religion. Christians constitute only about one percent of the population, and adherents of other religions are even fewer. However, both Buddhism and Shinto have, over time, lost their inner substance, become so commonplace as to no longer draw special attention, or been adapted to serve purposes unrelated to their original spiritual intent — and as a result, they have lost their vitality. For most people, their engagement with religion is limited to customary, incidental, or nominal participation in events such as life-cycle ceremonies (births, coming-of-age celebrations, weddings, anniversaries, funerals, and memorial visits), sightseeing at shrines and temples, or community festivals centered around religious sites — all of which are largely detached from the original spiritual aims of religion.

Shinto is a polytheistic tradition that holds that deities dwell in all natural things — seas, mountains, trees, and so on. In contrast, the central figure of Buddhism is not a god, but the Buddha — a human being who attained enlightenment.

Buddhism places the concept of causality at its core, as seen in its doctrine of karmic retribution. According to the definition in §1: ''The Universe and God,'' which identifies divine will with causality, Buddhism does not revere a deity as such, but rather venerates divine will itself.

A central tenet of Buddhism is the belief in past, present, and future lives. Thus, karmic teachings assert that ''misfortune in this life is the result of evil deeds in a past life,'' or that ''evil deeds in this life will bring misfortune in the next.'' In other words, the former posits a causal connection between ''committing evil in a past life'' and ''suffering misfortune in the present,'' while the latter posits a causal connection between ''committing evil in the present'' and ''suffering misfortune in the next life.'' Though such claims may seem fictional today, they were perhaps the products of earnest speculation in the past about causality.

The teaching ''If you commit evil in this life, you will suffer in the next'' implies its converse: ''If you wish to be happy in the next life, perform good deeds in this one.'' Since good deeds align with morally appropriate conduct, this teaching connects to ethics. In monotheistic religions such as Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, sacred texts are often regarded as sources of ethical norms. Even when these norms are presented as divine commandments, they are in fact based on ancient speculation about causality by unknown authors. Thus, religion and ethics often intermingle, sharing the feature of teaching and learning based on speculative understandings of causality.

In contrast to science and logic — which, as explained in §4.1 and §4.2, are grounded in evidential laws — ethics and religion often accept speculation as truth without demanding evidence, provided it comes from a teacher or authority. This marks a clear boundary between science and logic on one side, and ethics and religion on the other.

Religion, of course, possesses features not found in ethics. One such feature is prayer concerning causality. In Japan, people often offer coins at shrines or temples and pray, saying, ''Please let such-and-such happen.'' This is essentially a request for the deity to establish a causal connection between one's offering and the desired outcome. Implicit in this is the belief that the deity governs causality in some way. While the Buddha, as an enlightened human, may understand causality, he does not govern it — thus, praying to him for causal intervention is misguided. In Christianity, believers pray, ''Our Father in heaven, may such-and-such come to pass,'' and make offerings to the church. Again, this reflects the belief that God governs causality and can link one's offerings to desired outcomes. We do not pray about causality in studying ethics, much less in studying science and logic. Religious believers, moreover, often accept unfulfilled prayers without protest. Indeed, one who cannot accept unanswered prayers is scarcely considered a true believer.

Another feature of religion — not found in science, logic, or ethics — is its tendency to embellish itself in order to encourage believers to accept outcomes of speculation and prayer concerning causality. Such embellishment includes distinctive attire and furnishings, solemn rituals, festive ceremonies, magnificent temples, elegant gardens, majestic music, sacred art and sculpture, ornate scriptures, and political patronage. These elements stir the emotions of believers, subtly guiding them toward acceptance. Indeed, the very concept of a deity can be seen as a form of embellishment. In Buddhism, for example, such a divine figure holds little central importance. Buddhism does not center on a god, but rather on causality; its central figure is the Buddha — one who attained enlightenment concerning causality.

Some such embellishment — from the perspective of an outsider — may be aesthetically or spiritually uplifting, offering clarity, serenity, or healing. But because they appeal to emotion rather than intellect, they may not be beneficial for believers themselves. Islam prohibits images and statues, recognizing their potential to foster emotional idolatry. In Japan, the effectiveness of the fumi-e (Christian image trampling test) for the crackdown on Christianity during the Edo period demonstrated that idolatry can undermine true belief. Although Confucianism is better understood as ethics, its temples and historical political patronage reveal its less desirable religious aspects.

Even science and academia have minor embellishment. In many Western universities, graduates and professors wear academic gowns resembling clerical robes during ceremonies. While this may have been traditional in the past, it is now a peculiar custom. Such practices were rare in Japanese universities when I was a student, but have become more common — perhaps to appeal to international students.

Another problematic feature of religion — again, not found in science, logic, or ethics — is its tendency to infantilize believers in order to encourage acceptance of speculative teachings. This involves fostering a mindset in which even simplistic doctrines are accepted without question. For example, one Buddhist sect once taught: ''If one merely chants the name of Amida Buddha, then one will attain salvation and eternal life in the Pure Land.'' In Christianity, even today, believers recite creeds affirming: ''If the dead and the living are judged and forgiven by Jesus Christ, then they will be granted eternal life in the Kingdom of God.'' These are both teachings of causality — and yet, even as a child, I could not accept such simplistic claims. In Christianity, the doctrine of the Trinity — that Jesus was born of a virgin, died, resurrected, and ascended to heaven — is itself a childlike notion, and accepting it is the first threshold to becoming a believer. Such doctrines are not merely simplistic; they are irrational, that is, inconsistent with ratiocination, as defined in §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language. In today's world, where many people understand causality, irrationality often appears indistinguishable from childishness.

In contrast, Confucianism — and ethics more broadly — likely contains no such childish teachings. It is grounded in trust in human intellect, and, of course, so are science and logic. To interpret charitably, religious leaders who teach about eternal life in simplistic terms may not be seeking to infantilize believers, but rather may be driven by a sense of mission to alleviate fear of death by any means. Such an approach may have been acceptable in the premodern era, but in today's scientifically advanced world, it is misguided. Instead, we should teach a view of life and death grounded in biology, as explained in §3.3: ''Baton of Life.''

Another undesirable feature of religion — again, not found in science, logic, or ethics — is its tendency toward irrationality, stemming from its reliance on speculative teachings about causality. As discussed above, certain Buddhist and Christian doctrines exemplify this. Irrationality is problematic outside the realm of fiction, and when it appears in the scriptures or teachings of powerful religions, it can imply that ''faith means emotionally accepting even the irrational,'' which may have harmful consequences for society. Science as described in §4.1, is immune to irrationality because it seeks laws governing aspects of causality, supported by evidence. Logic, too, as explained in §4.2 is inherently opposed to irrationality, because it seeks to understand causality on the basis of logical laws. Although ethics also relies on speculation about causality, its moral orientation — as a teaching of morally appropriate conduct — likely protects it from irrationality. Religions that closely resemble ethics may be less prone to irrationality, and some may attract followers precisely because of this resemblance. However, some religions may exploit this resemblance to equate rejection of their teachings with immorality, thereby pressuring believers into accepting irrational doctrines through fear of being judged immoral. Such religions, if any, are degenerate religions.

Another problematic feature of religion is its ancient traditionalism — the continued adherence to scriptures and doctrines formulated in antiquity. All the religions discussed in this article originated in ancient times. Islam, though relatively recent, builds upon Christianity, which in turn builds upon Judaism. So-called new religions also tend to draw from ancient traditions. Religion is often seen as a direct descendant of prehistoric magic, and thus carries an inherent antiquity. The antiquity of religion is problematic because the ignorance of ancient peoples often gave rise to irrational doctrines, which, as discussed above, can lead to harmful consequences. Its traditionalism is problematic because it tends to suppress creativity and intellectual independence. While ethics may acquire traditionalism when it overlaps with religion, logic and science — needless to say — are free from such constraints.

As for science, we may state the following: We cannot accept some religious creeds of ancient origin without ignoring science of modern origin (and empirical knowledge). This may have caused a conflict between religion and science — or more precisely, between anciency and modernity, ignorance and intelligence, conventionality and creativity, emotional obedience and intellectual independence, and so on.

Another undesirable feature of religion — again, not found in science, logic, or ethics — is its political entanglement. Religious authority often legitimizes political actions, while political power, in turn, supports religion. This mutual dependence has allowed ancient religions to take root in society and persist into the present. In some modern nations, Islam still maintains a close integration with political power. In Japan, the assassination of former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe in July 2022 brought to light the ties between the Unification Church and the Liberal Democratic Party. There have been — and still are — instances where political parties use religious groups to gather votes, religious groups use political parties to recruit followers, or religious leaders use religion as a pretext to enter politics.

One of the most conspicuous problems unique to religion — and not found in science, logic, or ethics — is its propensity for violence. We rarely, if ever, hear of conflicts in science, logic, or ethics escalating into violence. In contrast, religious conflicts have often led to wars, strife, massacres, terrorism, and persecution. For example, the Christian world launched the Crusades as wars against the Islamic world. The mass murder of Jews by Nazi Germany was likely rooted, at least in part, in the long-standing antagonism between Christianity and Judaism. India and Pakistan, once united under British colonial rule, were divided upon independence due to tensions between Hinduism and Islam; this division led to violent conflicts and wars, and even today, the two nations remain in a state of nuclear-armed confrontation. In Europe, the Protestant Reformation gave rise to wars and hostilities between Catholic and Protestant regions, and even in recent times, Northern Ireland — part of the United Kingdom — has witnessed ongoing conflict and acts of terrorism. In contemporary Myanmar, a Buddhist-majority country, Muslim Rohingya have been persecuted and forced to flee as refugees to neighboring Bangladesh, a Muslim-majority nation. Similarly, the ongoing wars and conflicts between Israel and its neighboring countries are likely rooted in the enduring tensions between Judaism and Islam. At the root of such events often lies the belief that ''those of other faiths are evil.'' This is, in fact, a claim about causality — the idea that the event of holding a different faith is causally connected with the event of being evil. Moreover, underlying the belief that ''those of other faiths are evil'' is likely the intermingling between religion and ethics discussed above — the assumption that those who follow a different faith violate one's own moral code, and are therefore deemed evil.

There may well be other undesirable features of religion related to causality, but I will conclude my reflections here.

To summarize the features of religion related to causality discussed above — setting aside those that are undesirable, such as its intermingling with ethics, embellishment, infantilization, irrationality, ancient traditionalism, political entanglement, and propensity for violence — we may say the following: Religious teachers instruct their followers based on speculative understandings of causality. Believers accept these teachings without demanding evidence. They also offer prayers concerning causality, and accept the outcomes of such prayers, even when their wishes are not fulfilled. Believers inquire of the divine about causality, make requests, and offer thanks for divine consideration. Religious rituals that involve seeking answers from the divine are intended to convey speculative teachings about causality. Expressions of gratitude for divine responses to such inquiries or requests are signs of acceptance of the speculative teachings and the outcomes of prayer.

In this sense, religion is characterized by speculation, prayer, and acceptance concerning causality. This is the distinctive mode by which religion seeks to understand causality.

5.1. The Eusophic System of Science, Logic, Ethics, and Religion

Ancient traditionalism discussed in §4.4: ''Causality in Religion'' is not merely problematic; it is contrary to human dignity. According to Insights (7), humankind is distinguished from other species by its intellect. To cling to the doctrines of ancient religions, born in eras of underdeveloped civilization, is to deny this defining trait. Such denial stands in opposition to the very notion of human dignity, which demands respect for the value of our intellectual nature.

The new conception of God presented in §1: ''The Universe and God,'' — including the notions of omnipotence, omniscience, omnipresence, omnibenevolence, and providence — aligns with traditional conceptions in many respects, yet it also diverges from them in significant ways. Proposition on Causality in §1.4: ''Answer to Questions 1 and 3, and Much More'' suggests that the universe (noo-world) gives rise to God. Moreover, according to §1.2: ''Answer to Question 2,'' the universe was created by our intellect. Thus, God, too, is brought forth by our intellect. In other words, God does not descend upon us of His own accord, nor does He return at the end of the world; rather, God is something that our intellect brings into being. Therefore, what we must do — as beings endowed with such intellect — is not to cling to religions created by ancient peoples, but to establish a religion appropriate to our own age, propagate it, and practice it. We must believe that we possess the intellectual capacity to do so.

As discussed in §4.4, the essence of religion lies in speculation, prayer, and acceptance concerning causality. What, then, do speculation, prayer, and acceptance mean in the context of this new religion? The answer should already be clear. What we ought to pray for is the survival of humankind. What we ought to speculate about is the path toward the fulfillment of that prayer. And the purpose of such speculation is to discover an answer worthy of universal acceptance.

As stated also in §4.4, religion often embellishes itself. Our new religion should not embellish itself. The very concept of a deity can be seen as a form of embellishment. Buddhism, however, does not center on a god, but rather on causality; its central figure is the Buddha — a human being who attained enlightenment concerning causality. The core of our new religion should be a person who is revered for having attained a certain kind of enlightenment. What, then, is that enlightenment? §0.6: ''Conclusions on Eusophy and the Universal Question'' provides the answer: it is the realization that the supreme good lies in the survival of humankind, and that the highest human calling is to guide the establishment, propagation, and practice of Eusophy — the path of wisdom that seeks the way to humanity's continued existence.

In this connection, it is essential to recall the insight presented in §3.3: ''Baton of Life.'' Traditional religions have long been regarded as the primary source of life's meaning, offering narratives that explain why we exist and what we ought to live for. Yet contemporary biology reveals that life itself already contains a clear and universal meaning: every living being exists as a bearer of an inherited baton — a transmitter of life and knowledge constituting civilization to future generations. This scientific understanding does not diminish the role of religion; rather, it provides a new foundation for it. A religion appropriate to our age must therefore recognize that the meaning of life is not bestowed from beyond the universe, nor dictated by ancient doctrines, but arises from the very structure of life itself. The task of religion, in its renewed form, is to articulate this meaning, to cultivate the will to carry the baton forward, and to guide humanity in fulfilling this responsibility toward the future.

As stated in §0.6, Eusophy is the integration of science, logic, ethics, and the essence of religion as described above. The primary agents in the search for the path to human survival are science and logic. The role of religion — in its essential form — emerges precisely when science and logic fall short. This is where the necessity of religion lies: there are problems, such as global heating, where waiting for scientific or logical certainty may lead to irreversible consequences. In such cases, religion must step in to compensate for the limitations of science and logic, swiftly offering conclusions and entrusting their implementation to the appropriate domains. In Eusophy, ethics is the supreme ethics that regards the survival of humankind as the highest good. Its role is to bind together science, logic, and religion. Eusophy is likened to a fan: ethics is its pivot; science and logic are the main ribs, and religion the supporting ribs. We may call this structural integration the Eusophic Principle.

In accordance with this principle, our new religion must dispense with the external embellishments that characterized ancient traditions — priestly hierarchies, elaborate rituals, sacred vestments, and monumental temples. Such structures once served the social needs of early communities, but they do not belong to the essence of religion as understood in Eusophy. The core of religion lies not in ceremony but in intellect: in the prayer for humanity's survival, in the speculation that seeks its path, and in the acceptance that guides action amid uncertainty. Any external ornamentation would only obscure this essence and diminish the dignity of the intellect that defines our species.

5.2. The Common Origin of Science, Logic, Ethics, and Religion

According to the account given in §4, the four intellectual endeavors of the modern age — science, logic, ethics, and religion — all attempt to understand causality. The essence of religion lies in speculation, prayer, and acceptance concerning causality. Ethics is a discipline grounded in the speculation of actions that are morally appropriate — that is, actions causally connected with events deemed morally right. Logic seeks to understand causality through the laws governing the declaratives. Science, for its part, aims to discover, with evidential support, the laws that govern aspects of causality. Thus, science and logic share the common feature of seeking to understand causality through evidential laws.

How, then, did these endeavors to understand causality arise in the first place? This very question led to the conception of Eusophy. Since the emergence of such endeavors likely dates back to prehistoric times, we can only approach the matter through informed speculation. The foundation for such speculation — as well as for other foundational ideas — lies in what is declared in Declaration (6) under the title ''The Origin of Humankind'': namely, that humankind is a biological species, and that its origins are to be understood in light of the theory of evolution.

Evolutionary theory teaches that species survive by adapting to their environment. However, this may be more accurately expressed by saying that species survive by adapting to the biosphere. More precisely still, the term ''biosphere'' may be replaced with the ''bio-noo-world'' — that is, the noo-world constituted by the entities within the biosphere and the relations among them, as defined in terms of the causal intelligibility they afford. Delving further, we may restate the principle as follows: species survive by adapting to the causal structure of the bio-noo-world. For in the bio-noo-world, it is those species that adapt to its essential nature that survive — and the essential nature of any universe, or any noo-world, is causality.

Existing species have survived not necessarily by perceiving the existence of causality or consciously responding to it, but by adapting to it. In fact, the survival of a species requires the continued survival of a sufficient number of individual organisms, and animal individuals obtain the energy necessary for survival from food. For example, most spiders catch flying insects by spinning webs in the air. This should not be understood merely as an adaptation to the environment of open air where insects fly, but rather as an adaptation to the causal connection that, in such an environment, ''spinning a web'' leads to ''capturing flying insects.'' Of course, it is unlikely that spiders perceive this causal connection as such.

On the other hand, animals that perform tricks in zoos likely act with the understanding that ''if I respond in such-and-such a way to a certain human signal, I will receive food.'' This, too, should not be seen merely as adaptation to the zoo environment, but rather as behavior based on the perception of a causal connection — namely, that in this context, ''responding in a certain way to a given signal'' is causally connected with ''receiving food,'' and that the animal acts accordingly in response to this perception.

As we have seen, there are indeed a number of species capable of perceiving causal relations and responding to them. However, at some point, early humans — through some means — came to perceive the existence of causality more clearly than any other species, and moreover, began the intellectual endeavor of trying to understand it. For these early humans, whose survival was often threatened from the moment of birth, this must have been a matter of utmost urgency. The initial understanding of causality that humanity acquired through this primordial intellectual effort was neither precise, nor advanced, nor extensive. Yet even so, humans acted in response to that understanding, and through such action, they adapted to causality and thereby survived.

However, as human survival became more secure, that primordial intellectual endeavor gradually branched out — first into early forms of religion, ethics, logic, and science, depending on the different aspects of causality being understood and the differing modes of understanding them. Over time, these branches continued to evolve and further diverge, eventually giving rise to the modern forms of religion, ethics, logic, and science as described in §4. It may be surmised that this process ultimately led to conflicts between religions, tensions between religion and science, and the siloing of academic communities. The term ''siloed society'' refers to a social condition in which individuals or groups, like octopuses each secluded in their own pots, remain isolated and disengaged from one another.

Some readers may recall the view held by historians of science that science first emerged around the time of Galileo in the seventeenth century, and may therefore find the reference to ''early forms of science'' in the previous paragraph somewhat puzzling. However, such conventional accounts are likely based on a different conception of what constitutes the essence of science. In contrast, science as defined above — namely, the intellectual endeavor to discover, with evidential support, laws governing certain aspects of causality — and especially what was referred to in §4.1 as ''amateur science,'' can be understood to have existed since prehistoric times. For example, in a society that survived by hunting in groups, people would have come to recognize, through empirical evidence, laws such as ''cooperation enables the capture of larger prey'' or ''securing large prey allows the group to live more comfortably for longer periods.'' In this sense, they were already engaging in a form of science grounded in causal understanding.

Furthermore, early humans likely combined such causal laws to arrive at more complex inferences — for example, recognizing that ''if everyone cooperates, the group as a whole can live more comfortably for a longer time.'' Such inferences may be regarded as a form of primitive logic, as they involve reasoning akin to syllogistic structures. From this same causal law, normative principles may also have emerged — such as the imperative ''therefore, everyone ought to cooperate'' — based on the idea that actions contributing to the long-term well-being of the group are morally good. In this early form of social ethics, ''morally good'' would have meant ''conducive to the group's sustained well-being,'' and the act (event) of ''cooperating with others'' would have been inferred and taught as a morally appropriate behavior. Moreover, before going out to hunt, members of the group may have consulted an elder, asking questions such as ''Which direction should we go today to find large prey?'' or offered prayers like ''May we catch large prey today,'' and then acted according to the elder's predictions. Such a sequence of actions embodies the essence of religion — speculation, prayer, and acceptance concerning causality — and may thus be regarded as a form of primitive religious practice.

From this line of reasoning, one might even come to suspect that not only early humans, but also other species that hunt in groups — such as wolves, orcas, or chimpanzees — may possess rudimentary forms of religion, ethics, logic, and science. 6. Eusophic Sense of Crisis and Eusophic Value System

A sense of crisis can be explained in terms of the value origin event and anti-value introduced in §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language,'' as well as the perception system (PS), the ratiocination system (RS), and memory.

As stated in §3.1: ''Systems with Life and Survival Instinct,'' a living being is a system whose purpose is the continuation of its life — that is, the autonomous self-reproduction of its components, including itself. Accordingly, as described in §2.3, the event ''my life continues'' must be the original value origin for any living being.

Only when such a value origin is perceived, when the anti-values of various events are inferred, and when these inferences are retained in memory, can a sense of crisis arise. To cultivate such a sense, one must develop not only the perception and ratiocination systems, but also the causal graph that governs recall. In other words, one must develop the SS (Self) — as defined in §2.3.

For wild animals, the danger of death is ever-present. To lack a sense of crisis is to court immediate death. The risk of death tends to decrease with body size. An adult elephant or giraffe faces fewer threats than a mouse or a rabbit. Smaller or weaker animals can reduce their risk by forming large societies or herds. Among herbivores and small fish, many live in groups — perhaps for this very reason. Domesticated animals, including pets, likely possess a diminished sense of crisis. Their safety is ensured by their human caretakers. To become tame is, in essence, to lose one's sense of crisis toward humans.

What about humans? Young children, whose selves are not yet fully developed, are unaware of danger. They live in brightness, unburdened by fear. But as they grow, this changes. Even so, the human sense of crisis seems far weaker than that of wild animals. One reason may be that humans form large societies — societies that domesticate individuals, offer safety, and propose alternative value origins that are less urgent than ''my life continues.''

But I suspect there is a deeper cause. Adults possess a well-developed self. They know that they will die — perhaps by the age of one hundred. Yet unless they are at war, gravely ill, or condemned to death, few live in daily fear of dying. Indeed, to live each day, one cannot afford to be paralyzed by fear. To forget death is, in a sense, necessary for life. If this is true of death, how much more so for other dangers.

In short, it may be said that humans have evolved to weaken their sense of crisis. The severe cases of crisis-insensitivity described below cannot be explained otherwise. That is why I believe: we must strive to cultivate a sense of crisis — for the sake of the future.

7.1.2. Essence of Intellectual Exploration

Several more years passed, and a new realization came to me. It relates to Insight (4): ''Language reflects the mind, but the mind is also shaped by language.''

Intellectual explorers are those who pursue matters to their very roots. In Japanese, there are many words that express this idea of ''root'' or ''origin'': kon-gen (根元, 根源, 根原), kon-pon (根本), kigen (起源), kihon (基本), kiso (基礎), and so on. The characters used in these words carry rich metaphorical meanings: 根 (ne, root of grass) and 本 (moto, root of trees) evoke the roots of plants; 源 (minamoto) and 原 (hara) refer to the headwaters of rivers; 基 (motoi) and 礎 (ishizue) signify the foundations of buildings. All three sets of metaphors — roots, springs, and foundations — suggest a beginning (元, 起).

Indeed, in each case, the ''root'' is what comes first: plants begin from their roots, rivers begin from their sources, and buildings begin from their foundations. Thus, we Japanese — and likewise the people of China and Taiwan, who share the heritage of Chinese characters — are naturally inclined, when pursuing the essence of things, to follow this semantic guidance toward the beginning, to trace things back to their origins or to the distant past.

Judging from the English equivalents of these terms — root, origin, source, basis, foundation — it seems that Western thinkers, too, are drawn by similar linguistic cues toward the beginnings of things.

Indeed, those who study the origin of life, the phylogenetic tree of evolution, fossil hominins, or the behavior of modern apes (as close relatives of humans) are all, in one way or another, tracing backward — whether to the earliest forms of life or to the branching points of human evolution. (And I say ''tracing backward and downward'' because phylogenetic trees are often drawn with their roots at the top, inverting the usual orientation of trees.) Likewise, those who study elementary particles are pursuing the roots of matter, and in doing so, they too trace their inquiries back to the beginning of the universe and the distant past.

But this tendency to trace things back to their beginnings or to the distant past has both merits and dangers. Setting aside the merits for now, the danger is clear: it tends to obscure our orientation toward the future. And more critically, because there were no human beings (Homo sapiens) in the distant past, this backward gaze tends to obscure our orientation toward humanity itself.

Indeed, those who study elementary particles have, from the outset, lost sight of the human dimension. And those who study the origin of life, evolutionary trees, fossil hominins, or apes — even if they may have begun with a human-oriented perspective — seem to have lost it, or are in danger of losing it, either by venturing too far into the past or by becoming absorbed in peripheral matters.

The fact that no one has proposed an intellectual endeavor like Eusophy may be due, in part, to the tendency among those who pursue things to their ''roots'' to be guided by the semantic pull of such terms — to trace matters back to their beginnings or to the distant past, and in doing so, to lose sight of both the human and the future. They have long neglected the survival of humankind and the question, ''What should anyone do?'' — questions that concern the future of humanity.

To be fair, mathematicians are also among those who pursue things to their roots. Before I turned to mathematical psychology in ''MP,'' I too was, like most of them, traditionally — or rather, conventionally — focused on numbers, figures, and functions, and from the outset had lost sight of both the human and the future.

In Japan, China, and Taiwan in particular, many people have likely been misled by the term sūgaku (数学), a translation of ''mathematics'' that originated, perhaps, in the Tetsugaku Jii (Philosophical Lexicon, 1881), and which literally means ''the study of numbers.'' This etymology, though historically significant, is no longer appropriate for the present age.

However, in ''MP,'' as well as in ''MN'' and ''FE,'' I began with a human-oriented question — ''What is humanity?'' — and pursued not the roots of things, but their core. From there, I sought to extract their essence. In doing so, I was guided not by the metaphors of ''root'' or ''origin,'' but by those of ''core'' (核), ''heart'' (心), ''substance'' (質), ''nature'' (性), and ''life'' (生) — terms that suggest structure, center, spirit, content, and vitality.

This orientation allowed me to maintain both a human focus and a future focus, without forgetting the foundational insight that humankind is one among many species, without losing sight of the target: the survival of humankind, and with a sustained inquiry into the mind that ultimately led me to propose Eusophy.

Therefore, I have come to believe the following:

The true essence of intellectual exploration lies in preserving a human and future orientation, pursuing matters to their core, and extracting from that core their essence.

7.1.3. Extracting Essence of the Objects

The term ''extracting,'' as I use it here, refers to the act of discerning what is latent or implicit. It takes many forms and can be found throughout the intellectual endeavors of people across cultures and eras.

For example, from the earliest times, humankind has likely observed the plants and animals in its surroundings, gradually coming to recognize the commonality we now call ''life.'' From this recognition, people began to group all such beings together under the concept of ''living things.'' In doing so, they extracted the idea of life from the diversity of plants and animals, and then extracted the concept of living things from the totality of things. Life came to be seen as the essence of living beings, and the possession of life as their essential nature.

In this context, the pronoun ''what,'' as in ''what has life,'' functions as a marker of extraction. From the perspective of linguistic morphology — the study of the structural rules of language — such pronouns may be called nominalizers, as they turn declaratives (linguistic expressions referring to events) into nominals (linguistic expressions referring to entities). But from the perspective of linguistic semantics — the study of meaning — they may more aptly be called extractors, as they serve to extract certain entities from the totalitiy of entities.

The foundation of modern mathematics lies in the concept of the set, as explained in §1.5: ''Noological Set Theory.'' And the tool for forming sets is precisely this act of extraction. In this way, the semantics of language connects to mathematics through extractors and the concept of sets.

Grammar, too, is a discipline that extracts the structural rules of language as its essence. Its origins can be traced back to ancient Greece. However, from the perspective of ''MP'' and ''MN,'' the true essence of language lies not in its morphological rules, but in its semantic ones.

Likewise, ancient Greek philosophers recognized that reasoning — particularly in the form of syllogisms — played a central role in thought, and thus laid the foundations of logic. They extracted these logical laws as the essence of thinking itself. Ancient Greek grammar and logic eventually developed into modern mathematical logic, which now serves as an indispensable tool in AI engineering, in the semantics of language, and — more importantly — in answering the questions posed in ''MP,'' ''MN,'' and ''FE:'' What is intelligence? What is the universe? What is God?

In the seventeenth century, Newton explained the motion of the planets around the sun by means of the law of universal gravitation, using mathematics. In doing so, he extracted this law as the essence of planetary motion. In the eighteenth century, Linnaeus began the classification of living organisms, not by tracing each species back to its origin, but by extracting essence and essential nature — other than simply ''life'' or ''being alive'' — from plants and animals, and using these to classify them. In the nineteenth century, Darwin published ''On the Origin of Species,'' but he did not trace each species back to its root. Rather, he pursued the phenomenon of the rise and fall of species to its core, and from there extracted the law of evolution as its essence.

Indeed, not only Darwin, but all the figures mentioned above — whether explicitly or implicitly — pursued matters not to their origins or roots, but to their core, and from that core extracted their essence or essential nature.

The laws they extracted — such as the law of universal gravitation or the law of evolution — could not have been discovered through direct observation alone, even with the aid of telescopes, microscopes, or X-rays. Newton reasoned that just as an apple falls to the ground due to gravity, so too do the planets fall continuously toward the sun under gravity, and in doing so, orbit it. Darwin's reasoning is well known and widely taught in schools.

Thus, extraction includes not only observation with the physical eye, but also what we might call observation with the mind's eye — that is, reasoning. The examples of Newton and Darwin represent paradigmatic cases of such extraction.

There is a reason why mathematics appears so frequently in these explanations. Mathematics, in its original and proper sense, is not merely the study of numbers, shapes, or functions. Rather, it is a tool of mathematical science as discussed in §1.7: ''Mathematical Science and Models'' — a means of understanding phenomena by extracting mathematical concepts that express the core essence or essential nature of things, through observation and reasoning, and then analyzing them mathematically. Here, ''mathematical'' no longer means ''pertaining to numbers, shapes, or functions,'' but rather ''logical on the foundation of set-theoretic concepts.''

Earlier in this section, I wrote: ''Let us set aside, for now, the question of what constitutes the right equipment.'' Mathematics in the above sense, alongside empirical observation and experimentation in science, is surely one of the right pieces of equipment. Without acquiring and using mathematics, our intellectual endeavors risk being constrained — our hands and feet bound, our paths narrowed, misdirected, or needlessly prolonged. This is symbolized by the fact that even questions such as ''What is intelligence?'' ''What is the universe?'' ''What is God?'' can be answered with precision through mathematical science, as suggested in §1: ''The Universe and God,'' and §2: ''The Cosmos, Intellect, and Language.''

Philosophers, theologians, and scholars of religion ought to be those who can answer such questions with clarity. And yet, perhaps because they have distanced themselves from mathematics, they have largely failed to do so. Indeed, philosophers have long debated the existence of God — between theism, atheism, and agnosticism. While the seriousness of the question is undeniable, the fact that this debate has continued for over a thousand years suggests that it has been, in many ways, unproductive. Gödel, whose work I discussed in §2.4 in relation to the incompleteness theorem, the completeness theorem, and original sin, even attempted a logical proof of the existence of God. But this, too, has become the subject of further philosophical controversy.

To be fair, mathematicians — myself included, before I turned to mathematical psychology in ''MP'' — have largely remained bound by convention, focusing narrowly on numbers, shapes, and functions, and perhaps losing sight of the fact that mathematics was originally meant to serve as a tool for mathematical science.

There are many such unproductive paths. To avoid them — and to find our way out of them — I invite you to read on, and to learn about Eusophy.

7.1.4. An Alarm of Climate Scientists

Earlier, I wrote: ''this tendency to trace things back to their beginnings or to the distant past has both merits and dangers. Setting aside the merits for now...'' But in truth, the merits and significance of tracing things back to their origins or to the distant past must also be evaluated in terms of whether they serve a human-oriented and future-oriented perspective — that is, whether they contribute to the betterment of humanity's future.

A striking example of such merit and significance is the alarm that climate scientists have been sounding — what I referred to earlier as the Heating Alarm (3). This alarm can be elaborated as follows:

Alarm: Since the Industrial Revolution, which began in Britain in the latter half of the 18th century, humans have been burning fossil resources on a massive scale, releasing vast amounts of CO2 (carbon dioxide) into the atmosphere. As a result, the atmospheric levels of CO2 has risen to dangerous levels, leading to global heating. Historically, CO2 has been both emitted into and sequestered by the Earth's crust, oceans, and living organisms. Despite fluctuations, its atmospheric levels remained relatively stable over long periods. However, since the Industrial Revolution, the rate of human-induced CO2 emissions has far outpaced the Earth's natural capacity to reabsorb it, resulting in a net accumulation and consequent global heating. This rise constitutes a grave and immediate form of primary biospherical damage, and poses a serious threat to the survival of humankind in the near future. Therefore, global heating must be halted by ending the large-scale combustion of fossil resources and achieving net‑zero CO2 emissions. And at present, it is still possible to do so.

This alarm warrants further explanation.

First, the term fossil resources refers to underground materials derived from ancient life forms, such as coal, petroleum, and natural gas. These resources not only emit gases that cause global heating and atmospheric pollution when used as fuel, but also serve as raw materials for synthetic resins (plastics) and chemical fibers. When incinerated, these materials release gases; when discarded, worn down, or degraded, they release microplastics into the environment. These processes contribute to global heating and to the pollution of the atmosphere, soil, rivers, lakes, and oceans — forms of biospherical damage.

This is why, in this article, I often refer to coal, petroleum, and natural gas not as fossil fuels but as fossil resources. Terms such as carbon offsetting, carbon neutrality, and decarbonization are also commonly used to describe efforts to reduce emissions. However, I find the focus on ''carbon'' in such terms to be misleading. Carbon is not the problem. Unlike carbon dioxide (CO2), carbon itself is an essential element — indispensable to all living organisms, including humans, as a fundamental component of organic matter. It is carbon dioxide, not carbon, that must be identified as the true culprit.

My insistence on precise terminology as above stems from Insight (4). The mind is easily misled by inappropriate or suggestive language — whether one's own or that of others. This concern also connects to the next point.

It is also necessary to explain why global heating constitutes a grave and immediate form of primary biospherical damage. Terms such as ''climate change,'' ''climate crisis,'' ''climate emergency,'' ''climate catastrophe,'' ''climate dystopia,'' and ''climate apocalypse'' are often used in this context. While words like ''crisis,'' ''emergency,'' and ''catastrophe'' may be appropriate, I believe we should avoid using terms like ''climate'' and ''change.''

The term ''change'' carries the connotation of something that shifts on its own, which may lead people to believe that global heating is a natural phenomenon, unrelated to human activity. This undermines the acceptance of the scientific findings presented by climate researchers.

Likewise, the term ''climate'' tends to direct attention toward atmospheric and terrestrial phenomena — such as temperature, wind, and precipitation — while drawing attention away from the oceans. It also obscures the true nature of the problem, which cannot be neatly categorized under the label of ''climate.''

The real problem: Global heating ultimately leads to the depletion of surface resources — such as freshwater, arable land, fisheries, forests, and habitable land — and to increased health risks, thereby making our daily lives increasingly difficult.

The first issue with focusing solely on ''climate'' is that the oceans have absorbed a significant portion of the excess heat and CO2 from the atmosphere. Thus, global heating has likely affected the oceans — through rising temperatures, acidification, and shifts in marine ecosystems — even before its full effects are felt in the atmosphere or on land. Once the oceans reach their limits of absorption, the heat and CO2 they have stored will be released back into the atmosphere.

The second issue is that abnormal heatwaves in the atmosphere and oceans have already led to the rapid melting of glaciers, icebergs, and ice sheets in mountainous and polar regions. These are direct consequences of global heating. In addition, abnormal cold spells, droughts, torrential rains, and violent storms have become more frequent worldwide. These phenomena can be understood by analogy: just as heating a flask of water causes irregular changes in the temperature, density, and flow of the water and air inside, so too does global heating increase the turbulence and unpredictability of the Earth's atmospheric and oceanic systems.

As a result, surface resources are being depleted in real and measurable ways: water shortages for daily life and industry, declining agricultural and livestock productivity, collapsing fisheries, forest fires caused by dry air, landslides and floods from heavy rains, and the submergence of inhabited areas due to rising sea levels. According to the World Health Organization, heatstroke and other health hazards — both direct and indirect consequences of global heating — are also on the rise. All of these factors are making daily life more difficult for people around the world.

In this way, global heating and the biospherical changes it causes — both directly and indirectly — constitute a grave and immediate form of biospherical damage. Moreover, because global heating is the primary cause of many other forms of biospherical damage, it can be called a primary biospherical damage.

7.1.5. The Venusian Scenario

Global heating poses a serious threat to the survival of humankind in the near future. This is because of the following projection:

Projection: If we continue to burn fossil resources and release large quantities of CO2 into the atmosphere, the atmospheric levels of CO2 will continue to rise. As a result, the greenhouse effect will intensify, and global heating will accelerate. Eventually, the natural world will begin to release even greater quantities of greenhouse gases that it has long stored — gases such as water vapor, CO2, and methane, which are currently sequestered in the oceans, seabed hydrates, permafrost, and forests. This natural release, combined with ongoing heating, will create a vicious cycle in which heating begets further emissions, and those emissions, in turn, drive further heating. As this cycle repeats, the cumulative effect will be an accelerating rise in global temperature, ultimately rendering the Earth too hot for human habitation.

The term ''accelerating rise'' here refers to a runaway increase — an escalation beyond human control. Such runaway phenomena are not limited to global temperature. They can be either accelerating or non-accelerating, but in this case, the concern is specifically with accelerated runaway heating.

A vivid example of such escalation occurred in 2020, when the global spread of COVID-19 led to a sudden and dramatic spike in infection rates. Graphs depicting the number of infections showed a steep, upward curve — a textbook case of an accelerating runaway phenomenon.

The phrase ''too hot for human habitation'' brings to mind the planet Venus. Venus, often called Earth's ''sister planet,'' has an atmosphere composed almost entirely of CO2 and an extremely high surface pressure. As a result, its surface temperature reaches approximately 470°C, making it utterly uninhabitable for humans. While there may be other such planets, Venus is particularly evocative: it resembles a vision of Earth after a catastrophic escalation of global heating, a world where the biosphere has vanished in a blaze of heat.

For this reason, I refer to the above projection as the Venusian Scenario. It is one of the Apocalypse Scenarios, but it is an avoidable apocalypse — and therefore worth serious consideration.

In this scenario, the phrase ''greenhouse gases that it (the natural world) has long stored'' refers to substances such as water vapor, CO2, and methane, which are currently sequestered in the oceans, seabed hydrates, permafrost in the far north, and forests. The phrase ''natural release'' refers to the breakdown of these storage mechanisms due to rising ocean and air temperatures — increased evaporation, thawing of hydrates and permafrost, and more frequent forest fires due to drier air. There may be other mechanisms of natural storage and release, some of which remain unknown.

Among greenhouse gases, methane is particularly concerning. Although it receives less attention than water vapor or CO2, methane is stored in many of the same places. While its atmospheric lifetime is relatively short — on the order of a decade — its greenhouse effect is dozens of times stronger than that of CO2.

Thus, we must be concerned not only with CO2 but also with the atmospheric levels of methane. According to a graph published by the Japan Meteorological Agency in November 2023, titled ''Trends in Atmospheric Methane Levels,'' the global average levels of methane, after a brief plateau in the early 2000s, has been rising acceleratingly since around 2007.

The Venusian Scenario, when broken down through repeated syllogistic reasoning, reveals the underlying causal structure:

  1. If we continue to burn fossil resources and release large amounts of CO2, then the atmospheric levels of CO2 will continue to rise. If the atmospheric levels of CO2 continues to rise, the greenhouse effect will intensify, and global temperature will continue to rise. If global temperature continues to rise, the natural world will eventually release even greater quantities of stored greenhouse gases. If such gases are released in greater quantities, global temperature will rise further. And if global heatings further, such gases will be released in greater quantities. (This underlined portion repeats.)
  2. If this underlined portion repeats, the effect of ''global heatings further'' will accumulate. If this effect accumulates, global heating will accelerate. And if global heating accelerates, the Earth will, before long, become Venus-like — a planet too hot for human life.

At present, many countries around the world have set a goal of achieving net‑zero CO2 emissions by 2050. This is likely because these projections indicate that, without such action, global temperature will continue to rise uncontrollably.

Some critics argue that these projections are unreliable. However, current statistics show that the vast majority of climate scientists consider them to be valid. Even if we were to concede, for the sake of argument, that the probability of these projections being accurate is only 50%, we must weigh the consequences of two possibilities:

  • The harm of not acting on a valid projection, versus
  • The harm of acting on an invalid one.

In earlier times, when the Venusian Scenario had not yet been proposed, the balance between these two might have been debatable. But now, the situation is different. If the projection is valid and we fail to act, the worst-case outcome is the Venusian transformation of Earth — an irreversible catastrophe. If the projection is invalid and we act regardless, the worst-case outcome is economic loss from halting the large-scale combustion of fossil resources — a loss that is, in principle, reversible. Clearly, the former risk is far more severe.

Now, the surface temperature of Venus is approximately 470°C. This extreme heat is likely capped by certain physical laws. In the case of infectious diseases, such as COVID-19, even if the number of infections rises rapidly, the eventual increase in immunity leads to a peak and subsequent decline. Similarly, during the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake, the temperatures of the reactor cores at the Fukushima No.1 Nuclear Power Plant rose sharply, but eventually peaked and declined due to cooling efforts. An article titled ''Will Global Warming Run Out of Control?'' published by Japan's National Institute for Environmental Studies suggests the following:

While the mechanisms of natural greenhouse gas release — especially methane — are not yet fully understood, current research does not conclusively indicate that global temperature will continue to rise without limit.

From this, we may cautiously hope that even if global heatings rapidly for a time, it may eventually peak or decline for some reason. Indeed, we must hope so — for without such hope, there is no solace.

And yet, hope alone is not enough. What matters is where the temperature peaks or begins to decline. Even if it levels off at 470°C, like Venus, that would offer no salvation.

History offers grim reminders. During the Black Death in the mid-14th century, the number of infections soared uncontrollably. Before the peak was reached, countless lives were lost. According to a National Geographic Japan article dated May 4, 2020, titled ''The Black Death That Killed One-Third of Europe: A Historical Lesson,'' one-third of Europe's population perished. Such a peak offers no comfort.

The article quotes a historian who suggests that the plague helped usher in the Renaissance and modern European society. While this may reflect the historian's diachronic and holistic view of society, it seems to overlook the dignity of each individual life lost in the process.

Moreover, if we take an even broader historical view, we see that the Industrial Revolution — originating in modern European society — led to the large-scale combustion of fossil resources and massive CO2 emissions. This, in turn, has caused global heating, which now threatens the very survival of humankind.

In the case of the Fukushima No.1 Nuclear Power Plant, the reactor cores likely stopped heating only because they had already melted down — a catastrophic outcome. Thus, even that peak was a mixed result, not a true salvation.

As for global temperature, even a few degrees of rise beyond pre-industrial levels is enough to make human life difficult. In August 2024, for example, Japan experienced daily highs near 40°C across many regions. News reports warned of ''life-threatening heat,'' advised people to avoid non-essential outings, and predicted negative impacts on agriculture, as well as increased damage from typhoons and prolonged heavy rains.

Given that Japan's summer temperatures have already risen several degrees above pre-industrial levels, a few more degrees of heating — even if followed by a plateau — would offer no salvation. There is no time left to waste.

Some reports have described the current heat as a ''disaster-level heatwave.'' But this is not a natural disaster. It is a man-made calamity, a form of pollution caused by the massive combustion of fossil resources and the resulting CO2 emissions. And because this human activity can be halted by transforming society, the disaster can still be prevented.

Yet those at the helm of society do not appear to be taking the need for transformation seriously. One cannot help but ask, in all sincerity: ''What exactly do they intend to do?''

Only when we see the leaders of society take this crisis seriously — only when they show us, clearly and unequivocally, that they are committed to change — will we, the people, feel reassured and inspired to walk alongside them toward a better future. Like the people led by the goddess of liberty in Delacroix's painting of the French Revolution. What, then, is the goddess of social transformation? Tokyo Electric Power Company (TEPCO) has published a roadmap for the decommissioning of the plant, with completion targeted around 2051. Although the meltdown of three reactor cores was a grave disaster, the mere publication of this roadmap has brought a measure of reassurance.

We now await similar roadmaps from governments and from industries involved in power generation, manufacturing, and transportation — roadmaps toward the goal of net‑zero CO2 emissions by 2050. The United Kingdom and the state of California in the United States, for example, have announced bans on the sale of gasoline-powered and CO2-emitting vehicles by 2035. These announcements can be seen as parts of such roadmaps.

While we may hope that the ''goddess of social transformation'' will soon descend, the logic of the Venusian Scenario compels us to extend it as follows:

If we continue on our current path, global temperature will rise uncontrollably, and before it ever peaks or declines, the Earth will become too hot for human life.

As discussed above, it is a mistake to consider global temperature, infectious disease case counts, nuclear reactor core temperatures, and CO2 emissions in isolation. These phenomena are not separate events, but rather interconnected outcomes of a continuous chain of human actions.

In the mid-14th century, the Black Death swept across Europe. This pandemic, in turn, contributed to the emergence of the Renaissance and the birth of modern European society. That society gave rise to the Industrial Revolution, which led to the large-scale combustion of fossil resources and the massive release of CO2. This, in turn, caused global heating and the rapid advancement of science and technology. As a result, nuclear fission came to be used for weapons and power generation, leading to fears of nuclear war and the occurrence of catastrophic nuclear accidents.

While pandemics may appear to be natural disasters, they too are, in fact, rooted in human social behavior. Thus, all of these events — pandemics, global heating, nuclear risks — are not isolated incidents, but rather a single, continuous sequence of human-caused phenomena that now threaten the survival of our species.

Therefore, we must go beyond analyzing each issue in isolation. We must quantify not only infection rates, reactor core temperatures, CO2 emissions, and global temperature, but also the risks associated with the use of nuclear technology. Ultimately, we must develop a way to quantify the aggregate risk of human extinction — a measure of the threats to human survival that arise from our own actions.

The Doomsday Clock, maintained by the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, may be seen as the first attempt at such quantification. However, it remains largely a subjective measure.

Humanity has not yet developed an objective method for quantifying the threats to its own survival — threats that are, in essence, self-inflicted. And if we consider the continuous chain of events described above, it seems clear that this unknown self-extinction risk is steadily increasing.

This concludes the explanation of the climate scientists' alarm. What this alarm calls for is reflection on the past, recognition of the present, a sense of crisis about the future, and action grounded in all three. Specifically, it urges us to reflect on the past — that global heating has been caused by humanity's large-scale combustion of fossil resources and the resulting massive CO2 emissions; to recognize the present — that global heating constitutes a grave, immediate, and primary form of biospherical damage; to feel a sense of crisis about the future — that this heating threatens the survival of humankind in the near term; and to act — to halt the combustion of fossil resources and achieve net‑zero CO2 emissions.

This alarm traces the origins of global heating back to the time of the Industrial Revolution, using the methods of mathematical science to analyze the past. It also uses those same methods to forecast the near future, and based on those forecasts, it proposes concrete actions to be taken in the present. In doing so, it implicitly issues the following warning:

If we spend all our time looking backward, we may lose the future before we even understand the past.

In this form of climate science, I see a model — an ideal embodiment — of what Intellectual Exploration for the Future ought to be.
